## Headline

The right first pilot is narrow, cheap, and already half-built: two or three of Allan Marshall's real, already-demoed workflows (vehicle/fleet registration, treasury and investor reporting, meeting-to-decision capture excluding board and earnings-adjacent material), run for 90 days with Jarred embedded as a forward-deployed engineer, a signed baseline and kill criterion before day 1, and a hard wall between "the operational pilot" (should cost Allan close to nothing, an intentional loss-leader relationship investment) and "the $10 to 50 million ask" (a separate capital decision Allan makes after he sees the pilot work, not the price of the pilot itself). The single biggest risk that the general enterprise-AI-pilot literature does not cover, and that this specific pilot must solve before any capture goes live, is that Upexi is a NASDAQ-listed company: meeting capture and treasury-data handling around a public-company CEO carries real MNPI (material non-public information) and Reg FD exposure that the estate's own attribution/governance layer (currently the weakest pillar, self-scored strength 0 to 1) has never been built to handle.

### What 2025-2026 enterprise AI pilot research says

**Failure rates are high and well-documented.** MIT's NANDA-affiliated "GenAI Divide: State of AI in Business 2025" report (based on 150 leader interviews, a 350-employee survey, and 300 public deployments) found about 95 percent of generative AI pilots fail to reach measurable P&L impact within six months, and that externally-built or vendor-partnered tools succeed roughly twice as often as internal builds (about 67 percent versus one-third). Reported Aug 18 to 26, 2025 by Fortune and Forbes; methodology has been publicly contested (Marketing AI Institute), notably for defining "success" narrowly as post-pilot deployment with measurable KPIs within six months, and for basing the "zero return" finding on only 52 interviews. Treat the 95 percent figure directionally, not as a precise industry constant.
Sources: https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/ (Aug 18 2025), https://www.forbes.com/sites/jasonsnyder/2025/08/26/mit-finds-95-of-genai-pilots-fail-because-companies-avoid-friction/ (Aug 26 2025), https://www.marketingaiinstitute.com/blog/mit-study-ai-pilots (critique).

**Similar magnitude failure numbers recur across 2026 sources with different methodologies**, which is itself evidence the pattern is real even if the exact percentage is contested: Gartner is cited putting the enterprise-initiative stall rate at 63 percent before production, and separately forecasting more than 40 percent of agentic AI projects will be scrapped by 2027 and 60 percent will fail in 2026 specifically from a lack of AI-ready data. S&P Global reportedly found 42 percent of companies abandoned AI initiatives in 2025, double the prior year's rate, with about 46 percent of proofs-of-concept scrapped before reaching production. Only 31 percent of enterprises that have adopted AI agents run them in production despite 79 percent adoption in some form.
Sources (aggregated via search, original outlets not independently re-verified this pass): cited across https://initializeai.com/blog/how-to-design-a-30-60-day-ai-pilot , https://www.catalect.io/blog/the-90-day-enterprise-ai-deployment-roadmap-from-pilot-purgatory-to-production , https://www.ampcome.com/post/ai-agent-chief-of-staff-enterprise , https://paul-okhrem.com/enterprise-ai-agents-statistics-2026/ (all accessed 2026-07-08).

**What separates the successful minority.** A Stanford Digital Economy Lab paper studying 51 successful enterprise AI deployments (Pereira, Graylin, Brynjolfsson, published March 2026) found leadership involvement is decisive: the strongest cases had the CEO personally own the initiative rather than delegate it, with weekly check-ins that cleared bottlenecks quickly; 73 percent of successful deployments started deliberately small; 63 percent were explicitly framed as experiments, not commitments.
Source: https://digitaleconomy.stanford.edu/app/uploads/2026/03/EnterpriseAIPlaybook_PereiraGraylinBrynjolfsson.pdf (March 2026).

**A separate regression-based finding (cited via search, attributed to Bain) identifies four factors explaining 71 percent of the variance in payback speed**: eval/measurement spend above 15 percent of budget, a named executive sponsor at C-1 or above, a success metric defined at kickoff rather than retrofitted, and integration with the system of record. This converges cleanly with the Stanford finding and with the estate's own "graduated autonomy" instinct: name the sponsor, name the metric, measure before you build.
Source: cited via https://www.stackai.com/insights/measuring-enterprise-ai-success-the-essential-kpis-beyond-accuracy-for-scalable-impact (accessed 2026-07-08; primary Bain report not independently located this pass, flagged as secondhand).

**Time-to-value benchmarks.** Custom-built pilots take materially longer to show value than vendor/partner-delivered ones: one source (via search synthesis, original benchmark study not independently verified) cites custom builds averaging 89 to 118 days to first measurable value versus 29 to 41 days for vendor-delivered agents, and a lower pilot-to-production conversion rate (44 to 53 percent versus 66 to 74 percent). General enterprise pilots are commonly quoted at 8 to 16 weeks and $50,000 to $200,000 in cost; sprint-based, consultancy-led delivery models target first measurable impact within 60 to 90 days of engagement start, versus 6 to 18 months for traditional consulting engagements.
Sources (aggregated via search): https://helium42.com/blog/ai-implementation-roadmap , https://ssntpl.com/enterprise-ai-implementation-complete-2026-guide/ , https://initializeai.com/blog/how-to-design-a-30-60-day-ai-pilot (all accessed 2026-07-08, cross-cited industry figures, not primary research).

**The 30/60/90 phase structure is now a converged pattern across a dozen 2025-2026 vendor and consultancy playbooks.** Days 1 to 30: honest inventory, data/security/skills readiness assessment, a single-page go/no-go decision with a data-readiness score gate (one playbook proposes anything under 80 percent is a no-go). Days 31 to 60: build and pilot two or three high-value use cases in a contained environment against real (not synthetic) data; watch for a documented "day 35 pilot fatigue" stall point as demo excitement fades and technical complexity rises. Days 61 to 90: the inflection to production, embedding into daily workflow, extending to more users, with the explicit warning that letting more than 30 days pass between a completed pilot and the next approved initiative loses the momentum the pilot built.
Sources: https://www.velosio.com/blog/how-to-build-a-90-day-enterprise-ai-plan-the-ai-strategy-template-for-mid-market-leaders/ , https://www.baytechconsulting.com/blog/enterprise-ai-implementation-plan-90-day-roadmap , https://www.catalect.io/blog/the-90-day-enterprise-ai-deployment-roadmap-from-pilot-purgatory-to-production , https://initializeai.com/blog/how-to-design-a-30-60-day-ai-pilot (all accessed 2026-07-08).

**Instrumentation discipline.** Recurring advice across sources: set the measurement baseline before deployment (without it, no ROI claim is falsifiable); name 5 to 7 metrics up front split roughly as 2 technical, 2 business, 1 safety, 1 user/adoption; a named business owner and an agreed kill criterion (commonly "by week 8") are cited as the two traits shared by pilots that make it to production. Investors reportedly now expect positive ROI within six months or less.
Sources: https://agility-at-scale.com/ai/strategy/performance-metrics-and-kpis/ , https://www.mindstudio.ai/blog/ai-agent-success-metrics , https://initializeai.com/blog/how-to-design-a-30-60-day-ai-pilot (all accessed 2026-07-08).

**The forward-deployed engineer (FDE) model is the dominant 2025-2026 delivery pattern for exactly this kind of high-ambiguity, no-mature-product-yet, single-relationship pilot.** Palantir invented the role in 2003 to 2005 for CIA/Army deployments when it discovered implementation was a co-engineering problem, not a shipping problem; the "gravel road to paved highway" pattern (bespoke fixes for one customer, later generalized into product) is the direct precedent for how Jarred's own estate-to-client pattern already works (the Anchorage-pattern engagement scaffold reused for RoboStrategy is this same loop). FDE job postings rose over 800 percent from January to September 2025; Anthropic and OpenAI have both built FDE teams explicitly copying Palantir's model (OpenAI's team, formed early 2025 under Colin Jarvis, is cited as an example); FDE compensation is reported at $173K to $630K+, reflecting the hybrid engineering-plus-customer-empathy skill it requires. a16z's own caution is directly relevant here: the model only works if there is a real platform underneath the bespoke work; startups that copy only the embedded-engineer part end up as "Accenture for X" with no durable moat, which is precisely the estate's own stated worry about business-OS commoditization.
Sources: https://newsletter.pragmaticengineer.com/p/forward-deployed-engineers , https://a16z.com/the-palantirization-of-everything/ , https://www.mindstudio.ai/blog/palantir-forward-deployed-engineer-model-anthropic-openai , https://www.paraform.com/blog/forward-deployed-engineer-palantir-runway-greptile (April 2026) (all accessed 2026-07-08).

**Governance, not capability, is the story of 2026 enterprise AI.** The KPMG/Microsoft Agent 365 rollout to 276,000 staff (announced June 9, 2026) is explicitly framed by commentators as "governance is now the product": 2025 was about capability, 2026 is about control. This matters for the Allan pilot precisely because governance and attribution are the estate's weakest pillars.
Source: https://enterprisedna.co/resources/news/kpmg-microsoft-agent-365-enterprise-ai-agents-2026/ (accessed 2026-07-08, event dated June 9 2026).

**Competitive/pricing reference points for the "chief of staff" category.** No vendor markets a packaged "AI chief of staff for the CEO" product as of this search; the market instead positions comparable tools as enterprise search/assistant platforms. Glean (raised a $150M Series F at a $7.2B valuation, shipped a third-generation Assistant in February 2026) prices around $50 to $75 per user/month with a roughly 100-seat floor, putting typical large deployments at $240,000 to $480,000 a year; ServiceNow closed its $2.85B acquisition of Moveworks on December 15, 2025, with Moveworks-via-ServiceNow custom-priced at roughly $100,000 to $600,000+ a year depending on scope, deployment typically taking 8 to 16 weeks. Neither vendor publishes list prices. Separately, a human "AI Chief of Staff" executive role (not a product) has emerged at $200K to $350K base at Series B+ companies and large enterprises as of June 2026, reporting to the CEO or COO, with McKinsey, Accenture, Microsoft, Salesforce, and IBM cited as having created or formalized the title.
Sources: https://coworker.ai/blog/enterprise-ai-pricing-comparison-2026 , https://workativ.com/ai-agent/blog/glean-pricing , https://workativ.com/ai-agent/blog/moveworks-pricing , https://www.exploreagentic.ai/comparisons/moveworks-vs-glean/ , https://valueaddvc.com/blog/ai-chief-of-staff-what-the-role-looks-like-and-which-companies-are-hiring (June 2026) (all accessed 2026-07-08). None of these vendors sell what this pilot proposes to build (a single-executive, OKR-wired, gamified triage front door); they are cited here only as pricing and category context, not as a build-vs-buy option.

**Rigorous productivity baselines already sourced in the local estate (not re-verified by this pass, cited from the existing research packet at `research/inbox/allan-ai-deck/2026-07-06-productivity-evidence.md`):** RCT-measured gains cluster at 15 to 40 percent on in-scope tasks (Dell'Acqua et al., BCG/GPT-4 RCT, *Organization Science* 2025; Noy and Zhang, MIT, *Science* 2023; Cui et al., Microsoft/Accenture field RCTs); firm-level gains today run 5 to 15 percent (DX 2026 telemetry across 400+ engineering orgs, "10 percent, not 10x"); the honest end-to-end picture is task-level 20 to 60 percent controlled or 15 to 30 percent real-world, firm-level 5 to 15 percent, economy-wide about 1 percent. This is the correct evidence base to set expectations for Allan's own pilot metrics: promise 20 to 40 percent workflow-time reduction as the defensible range, not a multiplier claim.

## Options considered

**Option A: Broad, unscoped "be Allan's chief of staff" mandate from day one.** Rejected. This is precisely the failure pattern the 2025-2026 literature converges on: no named workflow, no pre-deployment baseline, no kill criterion, an amorphous mandate that cannot be measured. It matches the profile of the roughly 86 to 95 percent of pilots (depending on source and definition) that stall before reaching production.

**Option B: Buy an off-the-shelf enterprise AI assistant (Glean or Moveworks-class) rather than build.** Rejected for this specific pilot, though worth naming because MIT's own data favors buy/partner over build by roughly 2 to 1. These platforms are priced and shaped for large employee bases (Glean's floor is about 100 seats; typical deployments take 8 to 16 weeks and $100K to $600K+/year), the wrong shape for a single-executive pilot, and neither offers the OKR-wired scoring, gamified adoption, or attribution layer that is the actual product thesis being pitched to Allan. Using a third-party tool here would prove a generic AI-assistant thesis, not Jarred's own product.

**Option C: FDE-embedded narrow beachhead using 2 to 3 already-demoed real workflows.** Recommended. Matches the exact 2025-2026 playbook Palantir, Anthropic, and OpenAI are all running for high-ambiguity, no-mature-product-market situations, and matches the estate's actual asset base: Jarred already has two working demo pages built directly against Allan's stated pain points (fleet registration, treasury reporting) plus an existing trust relationship with Allan and Brian Rudick.

**Option D: Pricing structure: pure equity-for-overhaul (mirroring the Anchorage-floated term) versus cash-plus-equity versus a free or near-free first operational pilot separated from a later capital ask.** Recommended: treat the 90-day operational pilot itself as low or no cash cost, an explicit relationship investment consistent with the estate's own "the first one's free, classic addiction strategy" framing for the GTM ladder's first rung, and keep the $10 to 50M figure entirely separate as a company-level investment decision Allan makes only after seeing pilot results. Collapsing the two into one negotiation risks re-running the same unresolved cash-versus-equity ambiguity already open and undocumented on the Anchorage thread, a second time, with a second key account, before the first one is even settled.

## Recommendation with reasoning

Run a 90-day, single-executive pilot built around 2 to 3 named, already-proven-as-demos workflows, not an open-ended chief-of-staff mandate. Concretely:

**Days 1 to 30, Foundation.** Sign a one-page "success contract" before any building starts: named workflows (recommend vehicle/fleet registration and treasury/investor reporting as the two lead candidates, since both already have working demo pages and both are Allan's own stated pain points), a named business owner on Upexi's side (Brian Rudick, given the existing relationship), a pre-pilot timing baseline captured by hand for at least one full cycle of each workflow before any tool touches it, and a written kill criterion. In parallel, run a legal/compliance pass with Upexi counsel on meeting-capture consent and MNPI handling before any capture goes live; this gate has no equivalent in the generic literature and is the one piece of due diligence unique to a public-company client. Ship the front door in manual, read-only mode only: passive transcript tailing, triage cards, human approves everything, nothing auto-acts, consistent with the estate's own stated law from the shelved command-center design.

**Days 31 to 60, Build and prove.** Turn the Approve/Edit/Skip triage cards on against Allan's real inbox and real (non-board, non-earnings) meetings, reusing the already-built fieldy-clawdia and meeting-to-ClickUp extraction pattern rather than building anything new. Start logging the graduated-autonomy agreement rate (system-proposed action versus Allan's actual choice) on repeat decision classes; nothing crosses into auto-act this pilot, full stop, given the public-company stakes, but the logging itself is what earns the right to propose auto-act later. Hold weekly check-ins with Allan directly, mirroring the Stanford finding that CEO-level weekly involvement is the single strongest predictor of successful deployments in the 51-case study.

**Days 61 to 90, Decide and package.** Compare baseline versus pilot-period timing on the two to three named workflows, using the same honest-math discipline already proven out on the State of AI deck (sourced, dated, no overclaim; frame gains as a defensible 20 to 40 percent workflow-time reduction, not a multiplier). Treat day 90 as a launch, not a conclusion: have the expansion proposal ready before the pilot ends, since the literature is explicit that letting momentum lapse for 30-plus days after a completed pilot kills the follow-through.

**Staffing:** Jarred as the embedded FDE and relationship owner, full stop; this role cannot be delegated given the existing trust cadence with Allan and Brian Rudick, and the literature's own finding that a named C-1-or-above sponsor plus an external-partner delivery model explain most of the variance in successful outcomes. Pull in Carter Razink specifically for security/isolation hardening before any live client inbox or treasury data touches the estate (the estate's own "untrusted-content discipline" law demands this, and it is real engineering work, not busywork). Do not pull in Nick Metzler for this pilot; leagues, anti-abuse mechanics, and per-person scoring have no object to act on in a single-executive account, and his 7/31 mechanics spec is correctly targeted at the later whole-org tier, not this one. Add one explicit new role this pilot currently has nobody assigned to: a named external counsel or compliance reviewer for the MNPI/capture gate, engaged lightly (a review, not a hire) rather than attempting to build a governance product in 90 days.

**Expansion into the engineering-department tier:** Do not assume Upexi itself is the vehicle for stage two of the GTM ladder. Upexi's public profile (a small-cap digital-asset-treasury company plus a legacy consumer-products brand business) does not show a large internal engineering organization to sell into. Two honest paths forward instead: (a) reinterpret "stage two" for this specific account as Upexi's small finance/ops/treasury team around Allan, rather than literally an engineering department, or (b) treat a successful Upexi pilot as the reference case and talk-track that then unlocks a different account that actually has an engineering department, while Allan's own expansion becomes a capital relationship (the $10 to 50M conversation) rather than a headcount-expansion one.

## Risks to this recommendation

**MNPI and Reg FD exposure is the risk this recommendation cares most about, and it is unique to this account.** A NASDAQ-listed CEO's meetings, treasury transaction timing, and share buyback activity are exactly the kind of material the SEC treats as non-public and insider-trading-sensitive. The estate's own governance and attribution layer is its weakest pillar (self-scored strength 0 to 1 across the board in the source overview), so this pilot would be operating its most safety-critical constraint on the thinnest built foundation in the whole system. Mitigation proposed above (counsel review before capture, explicit exclusion of board/earnings-adjacent meetings in phase 1) reduces but does not eliminate this; it should be treated as a gating item Jarred personally signs off on, not a checkbox.

**Existing-relationship contamination.** Allan already pays Jarred for a separate, unrelated ghostwriting and capital-strategy advisory engagement. A pilot that underdelivers, or that creates any compliance friction, risks damaging a real, currently-healthy revenue relationship that has nothing to do with the Agent OS pitch. The estate's own stated law ("client experience must never degrade") applies with extra force here because there are two relationships to protect, not one.

**Base-rate risk is real regardless of design quality.** Even the best-designed pilot is competing against a 2025-2026 base rate where a large majority of enterprise AI pilots (estimates range widely by source and definition, roughly 42 to 95 percent) do not reach production or measurable ROI. Naming a kill criterion up front is a mitigation for Jarred's own decision-making, not a guarantee the pilot succeeds; it should be presented to Allan as intellectual honesty (matching the deck's own "honest math" discipline), not as a hedge that undercuts the pitch.

**Timeline collision.** The pitch itself lands 7/17-18, in the same window the overview already flags as a capacity collision with Anchorage's Sprint 1 to 2 work (7/9 through 7/27). A 90-day pilot starting immediately after the pitch would run through roughly mid-October, overlapping both Carter's 7/15 case-study deliverable and Nick's 7/31 mechanics spec, either of which the pilot's own talk track might want to cite as it develops. Capacity planning across these three tracks (Anchorage delivery, Allan pilot, and the wider Agent OS concept-doc work) is not resolved anywhere in the source material and should be before committing to the 30/60/90 dates above.

**The "engineering department" stage of the GTM ladder may not fit this specific account**, as detailed in the recommendation. If this is not caught explicitly, the natural next step after a successful Upexi pilot (sell into Upexi's engineering department) will simply not exist to sell into, and the expansion plan will stall for a reason that has nothing to do with pilot quality.

**Optics risk of the capital ask.** The product being pitched for $10 to 50M has, by its own scorecard, zero built multi-tenancy, zero built attribution layer, and a P0-requirement average strength around 3.4 out of 5. Recommending that the pilot and the capital ask be kept strictly separate (Option D above) is partly a mitigation for this: it lets Allan invest in a proof he has personally seen work at small scale, rather than being asked to underwrite a roadmap. If the 7/17-18 pitch conflates "here is a working pilot" with "here is a finished product," that gap is likely to surface as a hard question in the room.
