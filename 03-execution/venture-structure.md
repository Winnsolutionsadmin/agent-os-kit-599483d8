### What the web research says (all 2025-2026 sources unless noted)

**Equity splits with unequal, IP-heavy contributions.** The most cited structured approach is a weighted scorecard (often called a 6-factor model: experience, commitment, idea/IP origination, capital invested, personal risk, specialized expertise), scored per founder and converted to a percentage, specifically recommended for teams with asymmetric contributions rather than defaulting to equal splits (icanpitch.com, Sept 2025 update). Market data: equal 2-founder splits rose from 31.5% (2015) to 45.9% (2024), and equal 3-founder splits rose from 12.1% to 26.9% over the same period, but a majority of teams still choose unequal splits, and the median gap on unequal splits narrowed to roughly 51/49 (CRV 2026 founder guide; Promise Legal, Jan 2025). Sources are consistent that a founder who originated the core, working IP justifies a 60/40 to 70/30 tilt in their favor relative to a "blank slate" split, with 10-20 points of difference cited as meaningful without treating the minority founder like an employee.

**License vs. assignment for pre-existing IP.** Two structural options recur across sources (analaw.com; WilmerHale Launch): (1) fold the IP's value into the upfront split (e.g., 60-40 instead of 50-50) and then assign it cleanly to the company, or (2) have the founder retain ownership and grant the company an exclusive, perpetual, royalty-bearing license. Every practitioner-facing source treats assignment as the standard investors expect: institutional investors and acquirers require confirmation that all founder IP has been properly assigned as a condition of closing, and unresolved chain-of-title is one of the most common deal-killers in diligence. A minority academic view (Ganor, University of Iowa Journal of Corporation Law) argues licensing better protects a founder's downside if the company fails, and that bankruptcy law specifically shields IP licensees, which is the honest counter-argument to full assignment. Drafting mechanics matter: use present-tense "hereby assigns" language (not "agrees to assign," which courts have read as a mere future promise), pay real consideration (founder stock, not a gift), and file a Section 83(b) election within 30 days of the stock grant tied to the IP assignment.

**Consulting-vs-product separation (background IP / foreground IP).** This is the standard mechanism software consultancies use for exactly Jarred's situation: a "background IP" schedule lists everything a party owned before the engagement or developed independently of it (methods, tools, code patterns, know-how), which stays with its original owner; "foreground IP" is what gets created specifically for a given client engagement, which the client typically owns or licenses. A consulting company's ongoing refinements to its own reusable platform, made incidentally while doing client work, are the classic scenario the clause is built for, and the standard drafting move is to carve out of any client assignment "IP not specifically or exclusively related to the subject matter of the services agreement" (Practical Law UK guidance; bytesnap.com 2025 background-vs-foreground primer). The broader corporate-structuring literature (Mintz, June 2025; Sprintlaw UK, 2025) recommends a genuine intercompany IP license (not a handshake) between a product entity and a services entity under common ownership, priced at arm's length for tax and transfer-pricing reasons, with real governance separation (distinct board actions, not just shared personnel) so a court won't pierce the structure later.

**Vesting.** Universally recommended regardless of how the initial split is set: 4-year vesting with a 1-year cliff (25% at year one, then monthly over 3 years), the same schedule for every founder no matter how the percentages differ, because vesting (not the split itself) is what actually protects the team if someone leaves early (Pulley; Terms.Law Feb 2025 generator; Promise Legal). Double-trigger acceleration (vesting accelerates only if there is both a change of control and a termination-without-cause or constructive termination within roughly 9-18 months of the deal) is now the market standard for founders; single-trigger acceleration is rare because acquirers price it into the deal or negotiate it away at closing (Morrison Foerster ScaleUp guide; Crowley Law; EquityList).

**Conflicts of interest and IP contamination from existing client work.** The research on "consultant becomes founder" situations identifies three separable risk buckets that map directly onto this deal: (1) IP ownership contamination, where any existing client or prior-employer agreement with an invention-assignment or "improvements" clause can sweep in IP that was developed or refined while servicing that client, regardless of whether it was built on company time; (2) policy/contract violations independent of IP (e.g., an engagement's exclusivity or non-solicitation terms), which is a distinct legal theory from IP ownership; and (3) undisclosed conflict of interest, where a consultant pursuing a personal or affiliated financial interest (here: pitching a current $25k/mo-equivalent client for a $10-50M investment into a venture the consultant co-founded) is a textbook disclosure trigger under standard consulting-ethics guidance (Legal GPS; entrepreneuriallawadvisors.com; USOGE conflict-of-interest guidance). The universally recommended cure across sources is written, proactive disclosure at the earliest opportunity, not silence followed by hoping it doesn't come up in diligence. California-specific note: Labor Code Sections 2870 and 2872 limit how far an invention-assignment clause can reach and require the carve-out language to be physically attached to or referenced in the agreement, which is directly relevant to reviewing the existing Anchorage and Upexi/Allan agreements.

**Precedent: internal tooling becoming the venture.** Two of the most-cited startup origin stories are structurally identical to this one, an operator builds internal tooling to run their existing business, and the tooling turns out to be the real product. 37signals started in 1999 as a web design consultancy; when client organization broke down, the team built Basecamp for internal project management, clients started asking to use it themselves, it shipped in February 2004, and within about a year and a half Basecamp was generating more revenue than the design business, at which point the company dropped consulting entirely and became a pure software company (Wikipedia; Taskade; Jason Fried's own Medium account of the origin story). Slack's origin is the same shape from a different starting business: Tiny Speck was a gaming company (the game Glitch) that built an internal chat tool to coordinate a distributed team; when Glitch was shut down in December 2012, the company did not wind down, it pivoted entirely to the internal tool, renamed itself, and eventually sold to Salesforce for $27.7B in 2021 (TechCrunch, "The Slack origin story," 2019; buildingslack.com). The load-bearing detail for this dossier: in both of the most famous examples, the original business was not kept running in parallel indefinitely as a "separate but co-existing" entity. It was wound down or fully subordinated within roughly a year of the internal tool proving itself. That is a real data point against "keep Winn.solutions separate forever" as a permanent architecture, and for treating separateness as a deliberate, time-boxed phase with a decision point, not a default that quietly becomes permanent.

**Vendors and real costs for actually doing this fast.** Cooley GO (cooleygo.com) publishes free, law-firm-drafted Delaware incorporation packages (bylaws, initial board consent, restricted stock purchase agreement bundled with an IP assignment agreement, employee/founder confidential-information-and-invention-assignment agreement) that founders can fill in themselves; as of February 2025 the same document set powers Stripe Atlas's incorporation flow (Cooley LLP press release, Feb 21 2025). Clerky (clerky.com) is the paid equivalent that handles the actual Delaware filing end to end: $427 for company setup (includes expedited DE filing and first-year registered agent), $299 for a post-incorporation bundle that specifically includes founder Restricted Stock Purchase Agreements, pre-filled 83(b) elections, and Confidential Information and Invention Assignment Agreements, or $819 for a lifetime package covering both plus future SAFEs and hiring paperwork; typical turnaround is 2-3 business days for the filed certificate. Carta Launch (carta.com) is free cap-table software for companies with fewer than 25 stakeholders that have raised under $1M, which comfortably covers a 3-founder pre-seed cap table before the Allan round changes the picture.

## Options considered

1. **Full assignment, premium-adjusted split, standard vesting for all three (recommended below).** Jarred assigns his estate IP outright to NewCo at formation for a documented equity premium; Nick and Carter get forward-looking equity for committed roles; everyone vests on the same 4yr/1yr schedule. Cleanest for investor diligence and for Allan's team to underwrite in three weeks; the main cost is that Jarred gives up personal ownership of the IP if the venture fails or the partnership breaks down.

2. **License instead of assign.** Jarred keeps personal ownership of the estate IP and grants NewCo an exclusive, perpetual, license (royalty-bearing or royalty-free) in exchange for a smaller equity premium. Protects Jarred's downside and lets him keep using variants of his own tooling personally or for Winn.solutions regardless of NewCo's fate. The cost: this is exactly the structure institutional-style investors are trained to flag as a red flag (dependency on a founder personally, not the company), which is a real risk three weeks before a $10-50M conversation with Allan's team.

3. **Dynamic equity (Slicing Pie-style) for a short pre-formation period, converting to a fixed cap table before the pitch.** Track hours, cash, and IP contributions on a point system that self-adjusts until a triggering event (a priced round, or simply the 7/17-18 pitch), then freeze it into fixed percentages. Well-suited to genuinely uncertain, asymmetric early contributions (which this is, since Nick's and Carter's deliverables aren't done yet), and one attorney source claims it "virtually eliminates" early equity disputes versus conventional fixed splits. The practical problem here is calendar: there isn't really a clean multi-month pre-formation runway to track dynamically before Allan money potentially shows up; it would need to convert to a fixed cap table almost immediately, which mostly just delays the same conversation this dossier is trying to help resolve now.

4. **Two-entity structure with an explicit sunset/convergence decision point, modeled deliberately on the 37signals/Slack precedent.** Keep Winn.solutions as the vehicle for existing client retainers (Anchorage, Upexi, RoboStrategy-style engagements) under a documented intercompany services and background-IP license with NewCo, but set an explicit checkpoint (for example, 90 days post-pitch, or the close of NewCo's first paid pilot) to decide whether Winn.solutions' book migrates into NewCo, stays permanently separate, or winds down. This is close to Option 1 operationally but names the separateness as temporary-by-design rather than an unstated permanent assumption, which the historical precedent suggests is what actually happens either way.

## Recommendation (with reasoning)

Combine Option 1 and Option 4: assign the estate IP, split on a documented weighted framework, vest everyone the same way, and treat the Winn.solutions wall as time-boxed rather than permanent.

1. **Assign the core estate IP into NewCo at incorporation**, using present-tense "hereby assigns" language, paid for with founder stock (not licensed), with a Section 83(b) election filed within 30 days. This is what Allan's side will expect to see in diligence for a $10-50M check, and it removes the single-point-of-failure optics of the company depending on a license from one founder personally. Use Cooley GO's free Delaware incorporation package as the base documents, or Clerky ($427 setup + $299 post-incorporation bundle, ~2-3 business day turnaround) if you want someone else to actually push the Delaware filing and get the founder RSPAs, 83(b) elections, and IP assignment agreements done correctly without hand-drafting them under deadline pressure.

2. **Carve out a background-IP schedule specifically for Winn.solutions client work** (Anchorage, Upexi, RoboStrategy-style engagements, and any client-specific configs, data, or feedback-registry content) before assigning anything. This is the standard background-IP-vs-foreground-IP mechanism: NewCo gets the reusable platform (the OKR engine, the gamification engine, the fleet HUD patterns, the meeting-capture pipeline), Winn.solutions keeps the right to keep delivering its existing retainers without asking NewCo's permission every time, and NewCo doesn't inherit client-specific confidentiality obligations it never agreed to. Paper this as a short written intercompany agreement once both entities exist, priced at arm's length, even if it's simple.

3. **Set the initial split with the 6-factor scorecard, on paper, in the 7/11 doc itself**, not as an unstated gut call. Score each founder on idea/IP origination, existing working product, capital and risk already sunk, specialized expertise, and forward commitment. Given that Jarred is contributing a live, revenue-adjacent reference implementation (not just an idea) and the other two founders' core deliverables (the 16x case study, the mechanics spec) are both still pending as of this writing, an initial anchor in the 55-65% (Jarred) / 20-25% (Carter) / 10-15% (Nick) range is defensible under the frameworks above, understanding this is a starting anchor for a real conversation between the three of you, not a number this research can set for you. Write the reasoning down, not just the percentages: a documented rationale is what prevents the "40% of founders grow unhappy with their equity arrangement over time" outcome the Babson survey cites.

4. **Everyone vests on the identical 4-year schedule with a 1-year cliff from the same start date**, with double-trigger acceleration on a future acquisition, regardless of the unequal starting split. The premium for pre-existing IP goes into the size of the initial grant, not into a different or shorter vesting clock; this is the one point every source treats as non-negotiable, and it is also the fairness mechanism that makes an unequal split politically sustainable between the three of you.

5. **File prior-inventions carve-outs for all three founders before 7/11.** Carter should be able to keep authorship and reuse rights to Recurro as his own named framework (an input to NewCo's engineering pillar, not NewCo's property, matching how the overview already treats it); Nick should have the same protection for any pre-existing tokenomics or mechanics IP; Jarred should explicitly exclude Mima and any other out-of-scope personal ventures from the assignment schedule. This is standard PIIA practice and avoids a much harder conversation later about what any of you can walk away with.

6. **Disclose the Winn.solutions/Anchorage/Upexi dual role to Allan in writing before 7/17-18, and pull the actual Anchorage and Upexi engagement agreements now** to check for two things: an invention-assignment or "improvements" clause broad enough to sweep in the fleet-of-one tooling (checking against CA Labor Code 2870/2872 if so), and any exclusivity, non-solicitation, or most-favored-client language that could complicate pitching the same relationship for a $10-50M equity stake in a separate company. Approaching Allan for money while Winn.solutions is simultaneously his paid vendor is the single largest conflict-of-interest exposure in this plan, and the standard cure across every source is proactive written disclosure, not waiting for his counsel to find it. This also lines up with the estate's own already-flagged risk (32 pending Allan follow-ups, unresolved Anchorage pilot-thread status) that a clean approach to Allan requires closing loops first.

7. **Do not treat "keep Winn.solutions separate" as a permanent, unstated default.** Both 37signals and Tiny Speck (Basecamp, Slack) show that the internal-tool-becomes-the-company pattern historically resolved by winding down or fully subordinating the original business within about a year, not by running two businesses in parallel indefinitely. Put an explicit checkpoint on the calendar (suggest: 90 days after the Allan pitch, or whenever NewCo closes its first paid pilot, whichever comes first) to decide, on purpose, whether Winn.solutions' consulting book migrates into NewCo, stays permanently separate, or sunsets. Deciding this later by default is how the wall quietly erodes once NewCo has its own revenue, staff, and momentum.

## Risks to this recommendation

- **The percentages above are a generic-framework anchor, not a negotiated number.** Two of the three founders' concrete deliverables (Carter's packaged 16x case study, due 7/15; Nick's mechanics spec, due 7/31) do not exist yet as of this writing. Setting equity now partly prices in work that hasn't been de-risked, and either delivery could reasonably move the split after the fact; build in an explicit revisit point rather than treating the first split as final.
- **Full assignment removes Jarred's personal downside protection.** The academic counter-case for licensing (Ganor, Iowa J. Corp. Law) is real: if NewCo fails or the partnership falls apart, an assigned IP stays trapped in the entity rather than reverting to the person who built it over years. This is a genuine trade-off, not a settled question, and worth Jarred weighing deliberately rather than defaulting to "assignment is what investors want" without registering the cost.
- **Winn.solutions' own legal form is undocumented in the estate.** This whole recommendation assumes Winn.solutions can be treated as a clean, distinct going concern for intercompany-agreement purposes. If it is actually a sole proprietorship or a DBA rather than an LLC or corporation, the arm's-length intercompany license/services mechanism does not work the same way, and that needs to be confirmed (and possibly fixed) before any of the paperwork above gets drafted.
- **Disclosure to Allan could itself cost time the timeline doesn't have.** Raising the dual consulting/fundraising role in writing before 7/17-18 is the right call ethically and defensively, but if his side wants a cooling-off period, an independent review, or a restructured deal because of it, that collides directly with the compressed 7/11 to 7/18 schedule. Better to surface it now and absorb a possible delay than to have it surface later as a for-cause objection.
- **This is a framework map, not legal advice, and the estate's own plan already calls for a counsel pass (Track 4).** Nothing here should be signed as-is. Cooley GO's own documents carry an explicit disclaimer that their content is general information, not legal advice, and does not create an attorney-client relationship; the same caveat applies to everything in this dossier. Route this into that /counsel verification step rather than treating it as final.
- **Speed pressure across 7/11, 7/15, and 7/17-18 creates real incentive to skip structure to hit dates.** A safer middle path than either "fully structured now" or "handshake now, paper later" is a short interim memorandum of understanding that states the framework and split explicitly but is marked subject to definitive documentation, so the 7/11 doc can reflect real terms without requiring a fully executed Delaware entity in three days.
