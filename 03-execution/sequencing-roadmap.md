## Path-relevant external benchmarks (web research, 2025-2026)

**Seed-stage diligence has shifted from narrative to production proof.** For agentic-AI specifically, one dataset covering July 2025-June 2026 found the median seed round at ~$9.2M against a broader-market median seed post-money valuation of $24M in Q4 2025 (up from $18M a year earlier, per Carta/PitchBook-NVCA data), but "most $3M rounds now require at least 3-5 pilot programs with mid-market or enterprise customers" and investors are explicitly screening for "reasoning traces" (production logs of agent decisions) and unit economics after inference cost, not just a deck. [Causo Hub, "Raising a seed round for an AI agent startup in 2026"](https://hub.causo.ai/guides/raising-seed-for-ai-agent-startup-2026); [New Market Pitch, "Agentic AI Market Funding Trends (2026)"](https://newmarketpitch.com/blogs/news/agentic-ai-funding-trends). This matters directly: the Allan conversation as described (chief-of-staff pitch, $10-50M, equity-heavy) is closer to a strategic/strategic-investor deal than a priced institutional seed, so classic seed diligence norms may not bind, but if Jarred ever needs to syndicate that check with an institutional co-investor, today's product would not clear the bar on its own.

**Runway assumptions have lengthened.** The old 12-18 month runway rule is now "24-30 months minimum," driven by the seed-to-Series-A gap stretching to a median 696 days between rounds. [Puzzle.io, "Founders' Guide to Burn and Runway," Jan 2026](https://puzzle.io/blog/founders-guide-burn-rate-runway). Whatever check size is raised, model the spend against a 24-month horizon, not 12.

**Burn benchmarks for a team this size.** Pre-seed teams of 3-5 people typically burn $20-40K/month; seed-stage teams with a couple of hires run $50-100K/month (median ~$75K per Brex data, ~$80K per Carta). [Zeni.ai](https://www.zeni.ai/blog/average-burn-rate-for-startups); [Warp, "Startup Burn Rate Guide"](https://www.warp.co/blog/burn-rate-startup-guide); [WinSavvy, "Startup Burn Rate Averages by Funding Stage"](https://www.winsavvy.com/startup-burn-rate-averages-by-funding-stage/). Each additional engineering hire adds roughly $15-20K/month; a senior commercial hire adds $20-30K/month. Applied to this team (Jarred + Nick + Carter, all currently doing other things), a lean 3-person full-time push lands at the low end of that band before any new hires; adding 1-2 engineers to close the multi-tenancy/gamification backlog pushes it toward $50-70K/month.

**Enterprise AI pilots overwhelmingly do not convert, and conversion is expensive when it does.** IDC/Lenovo research found 88% of observed AI POCs never reach wide deployment; EPAM puts the figure near 80%; McKinsey's 2025 State of AI report found nearly two-thirds of organizations still stuck in pilot mode. [Agility at Scale, "Scaling AI from Pilots to Enterprise Deployment"](https://agility-at-scale.com/ai/strategy/scaling-ai-from-pilots-to-enterprise-wide-deployment/). When pilots do convert, the pilot-to-production jump typically costs 250-400% more than the pilot itself (data pipelines, security hardening, integration), and a well-scoped single-workflow pilot needs three to six months to reach a working production build, with function-wide rollout adding another six to twelve months beyond that. [AI Assembly Lines, "How Long Does AI Transformation Take?"](https://aiassemblylines.com/post/long-ai-transformation-take-mid-market-timeline-guide). Typical POC pricing for a 4-8 week engagement runs $25-100K. [Agentic AI Pricing, "How much should AI pilots cost?"](https://www.agenticaipricing.com/how-much-should-ai-pilots-cost/); [Coworker AI, "Enterprise AI Pricing Compared: 2026 Guide"](https://coworker.ai/blog/enterprise-ai-pricing-comparison-2026). Applied here: even a maximally smooth Anchorage or Upexi pilot is a Q4-2026-at-the-earliest production system, not a Q3 one, if it follows the industry pattern rather than beating it.

**The precedent that most closely validates consulting-first is Palantir's forward-deployed-engineer (FDE) model, and it is now the industry's explicit playbook.** Palantir embeds engineers directly in a client's operations, builds bespoke solutions on its own platform, and lets the engagement double as product R&D, "every engagement functioning as an R&D investment" rather than a one-off billable project. [Medium/Activated Thinker, "A Comprehensive Analysis of Palantir's Forward Deployed Engineering Model"](https://medium.com/activated-thinker/a-comprehensive-analysis-of-palantirs-forward-deployed-engineering-model-4502a036b5e4). 4. **It does not foreclose Option A.** Nothing about Option C stops the 7/15 deck or the 7/17-18 Allan pitch from happening exactly as scheduled, they were going to happen anyway, they cost nothing incremental, and if Allan writes a large check on relationship strength alone, that is a genuine upside surprise that accelerates Option C's roadmap rather than replacing it. None of these require the large, funded engineering team that only Option A's capital would justify. Option C can close all four gaps against real client data at a fraction of Option A's implied burn.

**Concretely, in the next 30 days:** keep 7/11, 7/15 and 7/17-18 exactly as scheduled (they're sunk-cost-prepared, per the overview's dated goals table); explicitly reframe the Allan meeting's ask from "fund the whole roadmap" to "become design partner #1 or #2 alongside Anchorage, cash-plus-equity, scoped pilot with success metrics", a strictly easier yes than an open-ended $10-50M platform bet; in parallel, task Carter (post his 7/15 case-study and 7/10 Recurro-doc deadlines) with wrapping a bought auth/multi-tenancy rail (WorkOS, given its SSO/SCIM lead) rather than building one from scratch, and task Nick's 7/31 mechanics spec with an explicit output contract: which parts of gamification get bought (Centrical/Mambo-class) versus which are the genuine differentiator worth building by hand.
