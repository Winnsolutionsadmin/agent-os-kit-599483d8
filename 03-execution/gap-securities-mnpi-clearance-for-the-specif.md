### 3. The legal mechanism that would make this permissible: the Reg FD confidentiality-agreement exception, layered under the broader (and more binding) temporary-insider and misappropriation doctrines

Regulation FD (17 CFR 243, adopted 2000) prohibits selective disclosure of material non-public information to a defined set of outside persons (broker-dealers and their associated persons, investment advisers, investment companies and their affiliates, and security holders under circumstances where it is reasonably foreseeable they would trade on the information) unless the issuer either discloses publicly at the same time, or first obtains the recipient's express agreement to keep the information confidential. SEC staff guidance is explicit that a general assurance not to misuse the information, or a bare trading restriction, does not satisfy this exception; the recipient must expressly agree to confidentiality.
Source: SEC, "Selective Disclosure and Insider Trading," sec.gov/rules-regulations/2000/08/selective-disclosure-insider-trading; Wikipedia, "Regulation Fair Disclosure," en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regulation_Fair_Disclosure; Vorys, "Regulation FD: A Refresher," vorys.com/publication-Regulation-FD-A-Refresher-on-the-SEC-Rules-Governing-Selective-Disclosure.

A precision note the estate should hold onto rather than paper over: Jarred, as a business consultant with no broker-dealer, investment-adviser, or securities-holder status, does not squarely sit inside Reg FD's own enumerated list of covered recipients. This means Reg FD itself may not be the single controlling rule for disclosure directly to him. What is controlling, and broader, is Section 10(b)/Rule 10b-5 and the "temporary insider" doctrine that originates in footnote 14 of Dirks v. SEC, 463 U.S. 646 (1983): a non-employee outsider (the Court's own examples are underwriters, accountants, lawyers, and consultants) who enters a special confidential relationship with an issuer and is given access to information solely for corporate purposes becomes a temporary insider, bound by the same "disclose or abstain" duty as an employee, for as long as that relationship and that information persist. Separately, the misappropriation theory (United States v. O'Hagan) extends insider trading liability to anyone who receives confidential business information in breach of a duty of trust and confidence and then trades on it or tips it, regardless of whether they are a classical "insider" at all. Practically, this means the confidentiality agreement the pilot needs is not merely a Reg FD box to check; it is the specific fact pattern (a special confidential relationship, access solely for corporate purposes) that determines whether Jarred is bound by insider trading law at all, and it is what protects Allan and Upexi if that relationship and agreement are ever scrutinized after the fact.
Sources: Dirks v. SEC, 463 U.S. 646 (1983), supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/463/646/; Katten Muchin Rosenman, "Insider Trading: A Primer," katten.com/Insider-Trading-A-Primer-10-26-2009; SEC, "Selective Disclosure and Insider Trading," sec.gov/rules-regulations/2000/08/selective-disclosure-insider-trading.

**What this means concretely:** before any real Upexi meeting or treasury data flows through Jarred's tooling, Upexi (through Allan, ideally with Brian Rudick and/or outside counsel involved, not just a verbal understanding between Jarred and Allan) needs an express, written confidentiality agreement specifically naming this new category of access, distinct from whatever governs the existing ghostwriting/capital-strategy-advisory engagement. A generic content-and-advisory engagement letter, written for a different purpose (drafting articles, Knowledge Panel work, capital-strategy conversation), is very unlikely to have contemplated an AI system that ingests, stores, cross-references, and potentially displays MNPI inside a broader multi-client operator estate, and should not be assumed to already cover it. Practically, this is also the moment to check whether Jarred is, in some functional sense, already a temporary insider today, given the existing capital-strategy-advisory relationship: if that engagement has ever exposed him to MNPI (financing terms, the Blueprint/Hivemind staking partnership before its announcement, etc.), the gap is not "does an insider relationship exist," it is "was it ever properly papered, and does that paper extend to an AI tool ingesting and storing the same category of information going forward."

### 4. Meeting capture carries its own, partially separate legal exposure, now with a live 2026 body of guidance

Beyond MNPI, the meeting-capture workflow specifically (Fieldy/Recall.ai/Otter-class tooling) carries two further, well-documented 2025-2026 risks:

- **Privilege waiver.** Mayer Brown's June 2026 client alert frames the core risk plainly: inputting privileged communications into a third-party AI notetaker may itself constitute disclosure to a third party, waiving privilege that would otherwise attach. Recent case law is unsettled and split, with three federal rulings on AI and privilege/work product inside a seven-week window: in United States v. Heppner (S.D.N.Y., bench ruling Feb 10 2026, written opinion Feb 17 2026, Judge Rakoff), the court declined to extend privilege to 31 documents a criminal defendant (Bradley Heppner, charged with securities fraud in connection with GWG Holdings) generated on the consumer version of a public generative-AI platform on his own initiative, without attorney direction; Heppner was convicted on all counts May 7, 2026, with his AI prompts used as trial evidence. The same day as the Heppner bench ruling, Warner v. Gilbarco, Inc. (E.D. Mich., Feb 10 2026) reached the opposite result on different facts, finding work-product protection can attach to material a pro se litigant prepared using a public AI chatbot where disclosure does not materially increase the likelihood an adversary obtains it. A third ruling, Morgan v. V2X, Inc. (D. Colo., Mar 30 2026, Magistrate Judge Dominguez Braswell), went further: work-product protection under Fed. R. Civ. P. 26(b)(3) extends to a pro se litigant's use of AI tools on confidential case material, though the litigant could still be compelled to disclose which AI tool he used; the court also held that parties may not upload confidential information to consumer AI tools unless the provider is contractually barred from training on it. The distinguishing thread across all three: represented-party, no-attorney-direction, consumer-tool use (Heppner) loses protection; pro se or work-product-only postures with a contractually-walled tool (Warner, Morgan) can retain it. This is directly relevant to the pilot's own tooling posture (see Finding 5): a no-training, access-controlled configuration is not just a security best practice, it is close to the fact pattern courts have actually credited when preserving protection. Any meeting where outside counsel is present, or where financing, M&A, or disclosure strategy is substantively discussed, is exactly the kind of meeting this risk targets, and is exactly the category `pilot-design.md` already recommends excluding from phase 1 capture (board and earnings-adjacent meetings). This dossier's addition: extend that same exclusion to any meeting where outside counsel participates, for the same underlying reason, not just board/earnings meetings.
Sources: Mayer Brown, "AI Notetakers: Productivity Tool or Emerging Legal Risk?" mayerbrown.com/en/insights/publications/2026/06/ai-notetakers-productivity-tool-or-emerging-legal-risk (June 2026); Cahill, "AI Note Takers in Corporate Meetings: Privilege, Discovery, and Governance Considerations," static.cahill.com/docs/CahillNXT%20Alert%20-%20A.I%20Note%20Takers%20in%20Corporate%20Meetings; White & Case, "When every word is recorded: AI meeting tools and the new governance risks," whitecase.com/insight-alert/when-every-word-recorded-ai-meeting-tools-and-new-governance-risks; Harvard Law Review, "United States v. Heppner," harvardlawreview.org/blog/2026/03/united-states-v-heppner/; ACEDS, "AI, Work Product, and the Protective Order Problem: What Morgan v. V2X, Inc. Means for Every Litigator," aceds.org/ai-work-product-and-the-protective-order-problem-what-morgan-v-v2x-inc-means-for-every-litigator-aceds-blog/; Kirkland & Ellis, "A Federal Court Charts a Path on AI, Protective Orders and Work Product in Discovery," kirkland.com/publications/kirkland-alert/2026/05/a-federal-court-charts-a-path-on-ai-protective-orders-and-work-product-in-discovery (all newly verified this pass, 2026-07-08).

- **Recording consent law, jurisdiction-specific to this deal.** Florida (Fla. Stat. 934.03) is an all-party ("two-party") consent state with no one-party citizen exception: recording an oral communication without every participant's consent is a third-degree felony, and illegally recorded material is almost always excluded as evidence. Upexi is based in Tampa. Any meeting-capture tool used with Allan, Brian Rudick, or other Upexi personnel physically in Florida needs actual, verifiable all-party consent for each session, not a generic "this meeting may be recorded" calendar-invite disclaimer, which multiple 2026 legal commentators explicitly warn is insufficient in all-party-consent states. This compounds directly with active, unresolved federal litigation against the vendor category itself: as of spring 2026, Otter.ai faces four consolidated federal lawsuits over recording participants without explicit consent, and Fireflies.ai faces two Illinois biometric-privacy suits. No court has yet held that running an AI notetaker is per se illegal, and no individual user has been held personally liable, but the live litigation means this is not settled ground to build on casually.
Sources: Florida Statute 934.03, leg.state.fl.us/statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&URL=0900-0999%2F0934%2FSections%2F0934.03.html; RecordingLaw.com, "Florida Recording Laws (2026): All-Party Consent Rules," recordinglaw.com/party-two-party-consent-states/florida-recording-laws/; tl;dv, "AI Meeting Recorder Lawsuits 2026," tldv.io/blog/ai-meeting-recorder-lawsuits/.

### 5. AI tooling itself is now a named, specific MNPI-leak vector in 2026 compliance guidance, and this pilot's own design has a metadata-leakage variant nobody has named yet

### 6. Regulation G and non-GAAP per-share rules add a distinct, filing-specific exposure if any AI-generated treasury output ever surfaces externally

Regulation G requires a reconciliation to the most directly comparable GAAP measure whenever a company (or someone acting on its behalf) publicly discloses a non-GAAP financial measure, in any medium, not only SEC filings. Item 10(e) of Regulation S-K layers additional requirements onto anything filed with the SEC or used in earnings releases: equal-or-greater prominence for the GAAP measure, and management's stated reasons the non-GAAP measure is useful. The specific edge case relevant here: non-GAAP measures presented on a per-share basis are permitted for performance measures (with reconciliation) but prohibited outright for liquidity measures, per Accounting Series Release 142, and the SEC's own C&DI 102.05 makes clear the staff looks at substance, not the label management gives a metric, when deciding which bucket it falls into. Upexi is already navigating this line in practice: it introduced an "adjusted SOL per share" metric specifically to supplement its basic figure, and MicroStrategy/Strategy has direct SEC comment-letter history on its own bitcoin-per-share disclosures (committing to consistent share-count methodology and naming its principal pricing market). The direct relevance to this pilot: the `treasury.html` demo already surfaces a "deployable cash" figure and a "SOL-per-share bridge," exactly the kind of metric that could be read as a liquidity measure if it ever appeared, with real numbers, in any externally-facing document, whether an actual Upexi filing, an investor deck, or even a screenshot shared outside a tightly controlled circle. This is a reason, independent of the MNPI/insider-trading analysis above, that real Upexi numbers should never appear in any pitch material shown to a third party (including a future Anchorage conversation, or any other prospective investor), only in fictional or already-public form.
Sources: Cooley CapitalXchange, "Non-GAAP Financial Metrics and Disclosures: Regulation G and Item 10(e)," capx.cooley.com/2025/04/22/non-gaap-financial-metrics-and-disclosures-regulation-g-and-item-10e-of-regulation-s-k/; Deloitte DART, "4.4 Non-GAAP Per-Share Measures," dart.deloitte.com/USDART/home/accounting/sec/sec-reporting-interpretations-manual/roadmap-non-gaap-financial-measures/chapter-4-non-gaap-measures-that/4-4-non-gaap-per-share; aggregated search of Upexi and Strive 8-K per-share disclosures and a MicroStrategy SEC comment-letter exchange (sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1050446/000119312521305105/filename1.htm).

### 7. A third-party AI/data-handling layer also touches Upexi's own cybersecurity disclosure obligations

SEC Item 106 of Regulation S-K (effective September 2023) requires public companies to describe, in their 10-K, their processes for overseeing risks from third-party service providers, and Item 1.05 requires an 8-K within four business days of a materiality determination for a cybersecurity incident, including incidents that occur on a third-party system: SEC guidance is explicit that "depending on the circumstances of an incident that occurs on a third-party system, disclosure may be required by both the service provider and the customer, or by one but not the other, or by neither." Once Upexi MNPI (meeting transcripts, treasury deal memos) lives on Jarred's own infrastructure, that infrastructure becomes part of what Upexi's own Item 106 disclosure and third-party risk oversight would, in principle, need to account for, and a security incident there becomes a candidate Item 1.05 event for Upexi, not just a Jarred-side problem. The estate's own scorecard already rates its attribution/governance/observability layer at strength 0 to 1, the weakest pillar system-wide; that gap is generic across every client, but it is highest-stakes here because the downside of a failure is not "an internal demo breaks," it is "a NASDAQ-listed company's cybersecurity disclosure obligations get triggered by a vendor most of its own investors have never heard of."
Sources: SEC, Item 106 of Regulation S-K, ecfr.gov/current/title-17/chapter-II/part-229/subpart-229.100/section-229.106; K&L Gates, "SEC Adopts Final Rules for Cybersecurity Disclosures," klgates.com/SEC-Adopts-Final-Rules-for-Cybersecurity-Disclosures-8-7-2023; Hunton, "An Update on SEC Cybersecurity Reporting," hunton.com/privacy-and-information-security-law/an-update-on-sec-cybersecurity-reporting.

## Risks to this recommendation

**This dossier is not a substitute for actual counsel review.** Every legal citation above is general federal securities and evidence law, not an opinion on Upexi's specific facts, its specific engagement letter language, or Florida-specific corporate practice; `pilot-design.md`'s own recommendation to run an actual legal/compliance pass with Upexi counsel before any capture goes live stands, and this dossier should sharpen the brief for that conversation, not replace it.

**Two SEC EDGAR primary-source fetches were blocked (HTTP 403) in this research pass**, for Upexi's Exhibit 19.1 insider trading policy and an 8-K exhibit with SOL-per-share figures, and were attempted again (still blocked, same 403) in a second verification pass the same day. The specific language and figures cited above are drawn from aggregated web search summaries of those filings, not independently re-verified against the primary HTML in either pass. Some corroboration exists from a second, independent source line in the second pass: the exact SOL-per-share figures (0.0339 SOL, $5.68 per share, 58.9 million shares as of August 4, 2025) were returned unprompted and consistently by a separate search query, and the Exhibit 19.1 provisions (CFO pre-clearance, 30-trading-day window opening two days after results, MNPI non-communication outside the company) were likewise returned consistently by a separate targeted search, plus the Form 4 purchase prices and dates ($2.07 x2, $1.80, matching to the cent) were independently corroborated via StockTitan and GuruFocus secondary filings coverage. This raises confidence the figures are accurate but still falls short of a primary-document read. Before this is relied on for anything client-facing, someone with working EDGAR access (a browser session, not this environment's outbound fetch, which was consistently blocked across multiple attempts) should pull both documents directly.

**Reg FD's precise applicability to a consultant like Jarred is a genuine gray area, flagged rather than resolved here.** The analysis above deliberately does not claim Reg FD itself squarely covers disclosure to Jarred (he is not a broker-dealer, investment adviser, fund, or securities holder); the practical recommendation (a written confidentiality agreement, temporary-insider treatment) rests more on Rule 10b-5, the temporary-insider doctrine, and the misappropriation theory, which are broader but also somewhat less mechanically bright-line than Reg FD's own enumerated-recipient test. Counsel may reach a different practical structure than an NDA once these are the actual operative doctrines rather than Reg FD.

**The "keep the treasury workflow fictional-only" recommendation has a real cost.** It removes one of Allan's two most concretely-demoed pain points from what can actually be piloted with real data in the first 90 days, which weakens the pilot's evidentiary value for that specific workflow (there will be no real before/after timing comparison for treasury reporting, only for fleet registration and meeting capture). This is presented as the right trade given the exposure, not a free one.

**This dossier assumes the fictional-data framing of `treasury.html` is legally sufficient on its own merits for pitch purposes; that assumption is not independently counsel-verified either.** A demo built in Upexi's visual language, using Upexi's real metric names (SOL per share, staking yield, deployable cash) even with invented numbers, could still create investor confusion or an implication of accuracy if presented carelessly; the talk track should be explicit, out loud, that all figures are illustrative, consistent with the deck's own existing honest-math discipline.

**Timeline pressure is the biggest practical risk to actually doing any of this.** The 7/11 shared doc, 7/15 concept doc and deck, and 7/17-18 pitch are eleven days out from this research pass, and a genuine counsel engagement (even a light one) takes real calendar time to schedule and complete. If the choice in practice becomes "skip the compliance pass or slip the pitch date," this dossier's recommendation is to keep the pitch date and skip nothing but the risky data, not to skip the compliance pass to hit the date.
