## Headline

The timing here is unusually sharp. The same twelve months this product plans to pitch and pilot in already produced a documented, almost exactly analogous failure: Amazon built an internal AI-usage leaderboard called KiroRank in 2026, employees gamed it within weeks by assigning agents pointless busywork to climb the board, and Amazon shut it down. In the same season, Uber, Meta, ServiceNow, and Microsoft blew through or capped their internal AI token budgets, which matters directly because "token-burn-vs-output" is this product's own proposed scoring axis. At the same time, a real wave of AI-adoption bonus programs launched and produced mixed but genuinely measurable results (Omnisend, Shoosmiths, Sanofi, Brex, IBM), giving a live comparison set of what holds up versus what collapses. Layered on top of all of this, the legal and HR compliance floor for exactly this kind of system (AI-driven scoring tied to pay or consequential employment decisions) hardened materially during 2025 and 2026 across the EU AI Act, Colorado, Illinois, New Jersey, and Germany's works-council regime.

None of this kills the gamification pillar. It does mean the two things the product's own scorecard already flags as unbuilt, per-person scoring with anti-abuse design (Str-2) and outcome-based rather than activity-based measurement (the self-described "vanity axis" problem in the-sage's own goals.md), are precisely the two things the evidence says must exist before a comp-linked league is safe to sell to a regulated client like Anchorage or pitch to a NASDAQ CEO like Allan Marshall. The single clearest, most falsifiable finding in this pass: outcome-linked incentive design held up (Omnisend) in the same window that pure usage-volume league design collapsed (Amazon), which is close to a matched natural experiment for exactly the design choice this product has not yet made.

## Findings

### 1. Real AI-adoption incentive programs, 2024-2026, with outcomes

- **Omnisend** tied quarterly raises (2-4%) directly to measured AI impact per employee or team. Revealed by Fortune on 2026-06-09. More than half of 246 staff qualified in Q1; the finance team's invoice automation rate moved from 36% to 85% and unlocked a raise; raises averaged 3%; cash incentives caused staff to spontaneously share prompt libraries and tutorials across nontechnical departments. This is the strongest positive case found because the metric is an outcome (automation rate moved a real number), not a usage count. Source: [Compt, "AI Stipends in 2026"](https://compt.io/blog/ai-stipends-examples/); [HR Grapevine, 2025-12-03](https://www.hrgrapevine.com/us/content/article/2025-12-03-firms-are-turning-to-cash-rewards-other-incentives-to-boost-employee-ai-use).
- **Shoosmiths** (UK law firm, roughly 1,300 staff) created a GBP 1 million (about $1.3M) bonus pool contingent on hitting 1 million Microsoft Copilot uses firm-wide in the fiscal year, framed internally as achievable if everyone used Copilot about four times a day. Executive Tony Randle reported the firm on track to hit the year-end goal. This is a pure usage-volume target, structurally the same shape as Amazon's KiroRank below, just without a competitive per-person leaderboard layered on top. Source: [Bloomberg, "Bosses Try to Drive AI Adoption With Bonus Pools, Spot Cash For Workers," 2025-12-02](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-02/bosses-try-to-drive-ai-adoption-with-bonus-pools-spot-cash-for-workers); [Staffing Industry Analysts](https://www.staffingindustry.com/news/global-daily-news/companies-try-bonuses-to-get-workers-to-use-ai).
- **Sanofi, Brex, IBM**: cash, points, and merchandise for AI-adoption behaviors. IBM's "BluePoints" program, an internal AI-innovation contest currency redeemable for electronics, appliances, or concert tickets, is a direct existing precedent for a points-economy/marketplace layer, and it has run as a recurring internal contest rather than a continuous league. Source: [Bloomberg, 2025-12-02](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-02/bosses-try-to-drive-ai-adoption-with-bonus-pools-spot-cash-for-workers).
- **1Mind** (CEO Amanda Kahlow): spot bonuses for employees who identify automation opportunities, with forward-vesting equity under consideration for anyone who "automates themselves out of a role." This is a small-company case, but it is a direct existence proof that the "reward not replace" contract this product is built on is something a real employer is already trying to pay out, not just a pitch line. Source: [Bloomberg, 2025-12-02](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-02/bosses-try-to-drive-ai-adoption-with-bonus-pools-spot-cash-for-workers).
- **Accenture and Coinbase**, by contrast, reportedly used threat framing ("adopt AI or risk your career") rather than reward framing. **Starbucks** pegged roughly a quarter of tech-team bonuses to AI adoption. These sit as a documented contrast set against the "reward, not replace" thesis this product is explicitly positioning against. Source: [Reejig, "Use AI, but not too much: the mandate is backfiring," summarizing a June 2026 Bloomberg report](https://resources.reejig.com/article/companies-push-employees-to-use-ai-just-not-too-much).
- **The reversal wave, mid-2026, same season as the incentive wave**: Walmart capped how much staff can use its in-house AI agent. Uber's CTO said the company had burned through its entire 2026 AI coding budget by April, driven by Claude Code (rolled out December 2025) at a reported $500-2,000 per engineer per month, and the company capped the tool at $1,500/engineer/month. ServiceNow exhausted its annual token allocation before mid-year. Meta warned roughly 6,000 staff it was on track to spend billions on internal AI use in 2026. Microsoft canceled internal Claude Code licenses across its Experiences and Devices division effective 2026-06-30 and moved people back to Copilot after token billing turned a flat seat license into an unpredictable bill. Direct relevance to this product: token burn is not a neutral, safe-to-gamify yardstick right now. It is the exact quantity multiple large employers just got burned by and are actively capping. A pitch or product that scores "token-burn-vs-output" needs a budget-ceiling story built in from day one. Source: [Reejig/Bloomberg summary](https://resources.reejig.com/article/companies-push-employees-to-use-ai-just-not-too-much); [IBTimes UK, "Meta Employees Burned $221M in AI Tokens in One Month"](https://www.ibtimes.co.uk/enterprise-ai-budgeted-management-1806370); [HRD/HCAmag, "AI tokens are exposing what companies got wrong about adoption"](https://www.hcamag.com/us/specialization/financial-wellness/ai-tokens-are-exposing-what-companies-got-wrong-about-adoption/580025).
- **Amazon KiroRank (2026)**: an internal leaderboard scoring employees on AI usage, explicitly built to gamify and reward adoption. Staff began "tokenmaxxing," assigning AI agents pointless busywork purely to climb the board, and reportedly used AI for tasks they could have done faster by hand. Amazon shut the leaderboard down. This is the single most directly analogous precedent found in this entire pass: a real enterprise gamified an AI-usage league in almost the exact shape being proposed here (leagues, a usage/token metric, competitive ranking, adoption reward), and it collapsed from the exact failure mode, Goodhart's law, that Nick Metzler's own anti-abuse instincts (attainment caps, outlier detection, role-adjustment, all from his 2026-06-30 call per the local swarm-gamification.md dossier) are designed to prevent. This is also exactly the gap the product's own scorecard rates Str-2 (meeting-stage design only) as of today. Source: [Blaze Media, "It only took weeks for AI usage to break the corporate piggy bank"](https://www.theblaze.com/tech/it-only-took-weeks-for-ai-usage-to-break-the-corporate-piggy-bank).
- **The disclosure penalty (Atlassian research, 2026)**: 94% of US knowledge workers report using AI at work, but in a controlled experiment, a peer who disclosed AI use was rated 10x lazier and was 24 percentage points less likely to be recommended for a high-visibility project, with identical output, versus a peer who did not disclose. This is a hidden headwind independent of scoring-mechanic design: a visible, scored AI-usage league asks people to make public a behavior that currently carries a private social penalty even when hidden from any formal system. Source: [Atlassian, "New research shows honesty about AI use at work is backfiring"](https://www.atlassian.com/blog/ai-at-work/new-research-shows-honesty-about-ai-use-at-work-is-backfiring).

### 2. Academic and practitioner literature on what backfires, matched against this product's design

- **Carnegie Mellon, Tepper School of Business**: a study published in *Ethics and Information Technology*, "When Work Becomes a Game: the Moral Costs of Gamified Labor," finds that workplace gamification (badges, points, leaderboards) can erode employee moral agency by replacing genuine professional values with metric-driven incentives, even while producing short-term productivity gains. Relevant because this product wants to gamify judgment-heavy knowledge work broadly, not just routine transaction volume. Source: [CMU Tepper School news](https://www.cmu.edu/tepper/news/stories/workplace-gamification-erodes-moral-agency).
- **Goodhart's law literature** (multiple 2025-2026 secondary sources): "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." The convergent mitigation across sources is to measure outcomes (shipped features, resolved tickets, moved KRs) rather than process or activity, and to diversify metrics rather than rely on one. This is the exact distinction the-sage's own goals.md already draws internally between "activity-alignment" (self-labeled the vanity axis) and "outcome-movement" (self-labeled the real axis, currently blocked on undefined KRs and an unreadable ClickUp data source), per the local swarm-gamification.md dossier. Source: [Trev de Vroome, "Goodhart's law and gamification of metrics"](https://tdevroome.medium.com/goodharts-law-and-gamification-of-metrics-ff697ac86575); [arXiv 1906.01577, "Gamification of Enterprise Systems: A Synthesis of Mechanics, Dynamics, and Risks"](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1906.01577).
- **Surveillance perception**: gamification that reads as monitoring undermines the autonomy that intrinsic motivation depends on (Oravec 2015, cited across current practitioner literature); enterprise-systems research recommends aggregated-only visibility for individual data rather than raw dashboards visible to managers, since manager-visible individual detail is what converts a game into surveillance in employees' eyes. Direct implication for a league UI: a score visible only to the employee themselves, with team-level aggregates visible upward, is a functionally and legally different object than a per-employee score visible to a manager. Source: [ResearchGate, "Uncovering the dark side of gamification at work"](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/344330163_Uncovering_the_dark_side_of_gamification_at_work_Impacts_on_engagement_and_well-being).
- **Frontline-service research (Hammedi et al., 2021, still the load-bearing citation in current secondary literature)**: competitive gamification increases stress, overparticipation, and demotivation specifically when employees feel they cannot catch the top performers, meaning a raw global leaderboard demotivates the median and long tail, which is most of any organization's headcount. Cited in: [SHRM, "Be Careful: Gamification at Work Can Go Very Wrong"](https://www.shrm.org/topics-tools/news/technology/careful-gamification-work-can-go-wrong).
- **The Disney "electronic whip" case**, still cited as the canonical cautionary tale in current practitioner writing: workers themselves renamed a gamified monitoring system once it read as control rather than recognition. Cited in: [SHRM](https://www.shrm.org/topics-tools/news/technology/careful-gamification-work-can-go-wrong).
- **Player-motivation research**, cited repeatedly across current sources: only about 10% of any population is primarily driven by competition and achievement; the other 90% respond better to social, cooperative, or mastery framing. A single global competitive leaderboard is designed for the minority segment.

### 3. Compliance constraints on output-based bonus pools and AI-usage scoring tied to pay

This is the section with the least local estate coverage; nothing in the seven product-sweep dossiers addresses HR/legal compliance directly, so treat everything below as new to the picture.

- **US wage law (FLSA)**: a DOL Opinion Letter (FLSA2026-2, January 2026) confirms that bonuses paid under a predetermined formula, which is what a points, league, or token-burn score necessarily is, are nondiscretionary and must be folded into the "regular rate" used for overtime calculations for any non-exempt employee. A comp-linked league score is not a free-floating perk; the moment it touches a non-exempt employee's pay, it is wage data with overtime math attached, and mislabeling it "discretionary" does not exempt it if the formula and criteria are pre-announced. This is a concrete, missable trap for any V1 pilot that includes hourly or non-exempt roles, which matters directly given the stated ambition to reward the whole org, not just engineers. Source: [DOL Fact Sheet #56C](https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd/fact-sheets/56c-bonuses); [Perkins Coie, "DOL Confirms Nondiscretionary Incentive Bonuses Must Be Included in the Regular Rate"](https://perkinscoie.com/insights/update/dol-confirms-nondiscretionary-incentive-bonuses-must-be-included-regular-rate); [Foley & Lardner, 2026-03](https://www.foley.com/insights/publications/2026/03/dol-reminds-employers-to-include-non-discretionary-bonuses-when-calculating-regular-rates-and-overtime-premiums-but-how/).
- **Algorithmic pay-equity and discrimination exposure**: ordinary anti-discrimination and equal-pay law applies to AI-scored compensation with no AI-specific exemption. One cited analysis of 500 AI vendors found 20 flagged as high risk for producing algorithmic wage discrimination, meaning regulators and researchers already track this vendor category as its own risk class. Source: [Equitable Growth, "How artificial intelligence uncouples hard work from fair wages through surveillance pay practices"](https://equitablegrowth.org/how-artificial-intelligence-uncouples-hard-work-from-fair-wages-through-surveillance-pay-practices-and-how-to-fix-it/); [Offit Kurman, "AI and Wage-Setting: Legal Risks and Compliance Strategies for Employers"](https://www.offitkurman.com/offit-kurman-blogs/ai%E2%80%91driven%E2%80%91wage%E2%80%91setting%E2%80%91legal%E2%80%91risks%E2%80%91and%E2%80%91compliance).
- **EU AI Act**: worker performance monitoring, task allocation, and promotion/termination-relevant AI systems are classified high-risk under Annex III. Deployers (the client enterprise, not this product's vendor) must inform workers and worker representatives before use, retain logs for at least six months, ensure human oversight, and report incidents. Full high-risk obligations were originally set for 2026-08-02. A Digital Omnibus deferral push (proposing a move to 2027-12-02) went through repeated trilogue rounds this spring without full agreement, and the Council and Parliament gave final approval to a simplification package on 2026-06-29 and 2026-06-16 respectively, but legal advisors are telling clients to keep preparing against the original date regardless of the deferral fight's outcome. Penalties: up to EUR 15 million or 3% of global turnover for high-risk noncompliance, up to EUR 35 million or 7% for a banned practice (emotion recognition in the workplace has been banned since February 2025). The Act applies extraterritorially if the system's output affects anyone located in the EU, regardless of where the deploying company or vendor is headquartered. Direct relevance: any client with EU employees or EU-facing operations puts a comp-linked scoring layer inside Annex III's scope the moment it goes multi-tenant. Source: [DLA Piper GENIE, "The Digital AI Omnibus"](https://knowledge.dlapiper.com/dlapiperknowledge/globalemploymentlatestdevelopments/2026/The-Digital-AI-Omnibus-Proposed-deferral-of-high-risk-AI-obligations-under-the-AI-Act); [PeopleGrip Partners, "EU AI Act & Employee Monitoring: HR Compliance Guide 2026"](https://www.peoplegrip-partners.com/post/eu-ai-act-employee-monitoring-hr-compliance-2026-en).
- **US state layer**: Colorado repealed its 2024 AI Act (SB 24-205, a broad duty-of-care and impact-assessment regime) and replaced it with SB 26-189, signed 2026-05-14, a narrower disclosure-and-recordkeeping regime covering "automated decision-making technology" that materially influences consequential employment decisions, explicitly including compensation. New effective date 2027-01-01: three-year recordkeeping, mandatory notice to affected workers, and a right to human review of adverse automated decisions. Illinois (HB 3773) and New Jersey (regulations effective 2025-12-15) separately prohibit disparate impact from automated employment tools regardless of intent; Texas's TRAIGA (effective 2026-01-01) requires intent, a materially lower bar. The legal standard for "does our scoring algorithm functionally set pay" is actively being rewritten mid-2026 and varies sharply by state; a V1 pilot's comp-linked mechanics need to be defensible under the strictest jurisdiction they will touch. Source: [Law and the Workplace, "Colorado's AI Law Gets Major Rewrite"](https://www.lawandtheworkplace.com/2026/05/colorados-ai-law-gets-major-rewrite-what-now-for-employers/); [Jackson Lewis](https://www.jacksonlewis.com/insights/colorados-new-ai-law-shifts-employer-accountability-system-individual-decision-level); [Perkins Coie, "Navigating the Growing Landscape of State AI Employment Bills and Laws"](https://perkinscoie.com/insights/update/navigating-growing-landscape-state-ai-employment-bills-and-laws-what-employers-need).
- **Litigation precedent**: *Mobley v. Workday* is an ongoing case establishing that a vendor providing a scoring or screening tool, not only the employer using it, can potentially be sued as an "agent" for disparate-impact outcomes. If this product ever licenses a scoring engine into client organizations, vendor-side liability for the algorithm itself is an open, active question, not settled law. Source: [Forbes, "Colorado's AI Act Rewrite Moves Forward"](https://www.forbes.com/sites/alonzomartinez/2026/05/01/colorados-ai-act-rewrite-moves-forward-a-new-draft-for-employers/) (cites Mobley in context).
- **Germany specifically** (relevant if any client, including a European-facing Anchorage relationship, has German operations or a works council, or if the product expands into Europe): BetrVG Section 87(1) No. 6 gives works councils a hard veto, not a consultation right, over "technical devices designed to monitor the behavior or performance of employees." Federal Labour Court case law since 1975 interprets this broadly: mere technical capability to monitor triggers the requirement, regardless of actual intent or use. A works council can block deployment outright, or force a negotiated works agreement (Betriebsvereinbarung); cooperative negotiations typically run four to eight weeks, contentious ones twelve or more, with a binding arbitration panel (Einigungsstelle) as the backstop. A league or scoring feature shipped into a client with a German works council is a multi-week co-determination negotiation before go-live, not a sales conversation. Source: [PeopleGrip Partners, "AI Monitoring Germany: BetrVG, BDSG & 2026 Compliance Roadmap"](https://www.peoplegrip-partners.com/post/blog-ai-employee-monitoring-germany-betrvg-bdsg-compliance-roadmap-2026); [FASTEC, "Software Introduction: Works Council Has Codetermination Rights"](https://www.fastec.de/en/news/software-introduction-works-council-has-codetermination-rights/).

### 4. League and anti-abuse design best practice

- **Server-side, tamper-proof scoring is foundational**: client-reported or self-reported usage is the first thing gamed in any gaming-industry precedent. This maps directly onto why token-burn derived from platform-side telemetry (Anthropic/API usage), rather than a self-reported usage log, is the structurally sound instinct, so long as the metric paired with it resists gaming (see next point). Source: [Game Developer, "Design and safety tips for leaderboard"](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/design-and-safety-tips-for-leaderboard).
- **Outcome-linked metrics resist gaming far better than pure volume metrics**: this is the strongest matched comparison in the whole pass. Omnisend's raise program (an outcome metric, automation rate moved from 36% to 85%) held up; Amazon's KiroRank (a pure usage-volume league) collapsed within months, in roughly the same eighteen-month window. Sources as cited above.
- **Concrete anti-abuse mechanics with gaming-industry precedent**: attainment caps paired with statistical-outlier review rather than a hard cutoff (matches Nick Metzler's own framing from the 2026-06-30 call, "90% never hit the cap, analyze the ones that do," per the local swarm-gamification.md dossier, and matches published anti-cheat guidance to flag outliers rather than punish everyone at the cap line); role- or cohort-adjusted baselines (matches skill-based matchmaking and tiered or percentile leaderboards, i.e., compare people to their own cohort, not the whole org); showing nearby rank rather than global rank (addresses the "can't catch the leader" demotivation finding from the Hammedi frontline-service research above); team-based competition over individual competition where feasible (also documented as reducing the stress and toxicity failure mode); opt-in rather than mandatory participation (the single most repeated mitigation across both gaming and enterprise sources for the surveillance-perception problem); aggregate-only manager-facing views with individual detail visible only to the employee (direct mitigation for the Oravec surveillance-trust finding above). Source: [ADL, "Online Gaming Leaderboard Best Practices"](https://www.adl.org/online-gaming-leaderboard/best-practices); [Leaderboarded, "Gaming leaderboards and toxic behavior"](https://leaderboarded.com/blog/posts/leaderboards-in-gaming/).
- **Escalation, not instant or opaque shutdown**: Call of Duty's published security and enforcement policy uses warnings and temporary restriction for first offenses, escalating penalties for repeat offenders, and reserves permanent bans for severe or repeated cases. This is a more defensible pattern for an HR context than Amazon's abrupt shutdown of KiroRank, particularly given the compliance layer above requires advance notice and a path to human review before any adverse action tied to compensation. Source: [Activision, "Call of Duty Security and Enforcement Policy"](https://support.activision.com/articles/call-of-duty-security-and-enforcement-policy).
- **Enterprise gamification vendor landscape**, for reference on build-versus-buy: Centrical (real-time performance management, microlearning, gamification, and coaching, enterprise and mid-market focused), Spinify (sales-gamification and leaderboard platform, partnered with Slack in 2025 for an "AI Hype-Man" bot), and Bunchball Nitro from BI Worldwide (system-agnostic enterprise gamification SDK integrating with CRM, LMS, and intranet systems) are the three enterprise-grade incumbents most repeatedly named. Reported pricing ranges from about $10-20 per user per month for SMB-focused tools (Gametize, base-tier Spinify) up to $50,000-$500,000+ per year for enterprise-grade deployments with deep CRM integration. None of these vendors were found to have a token-burn-vs-output or AI-adoption-specific scoring model; they are general sales/performance gamification platforms, which is itself a market-gap signal in this product's favor if AI-adoption scoring is the specific wedge. Source: [MarketGrowthReports, "Top 17 Employee Gamification Software Companies in 2026"](https://www.marketgrowthreports.com/blog/employee-gamification-software-companies-108); [Centrical platform page](https://centrical.com/platform/gamification/).

### 5. The 16x case-study claim: an external credibility check

- METR's own 2025 randomized controlled trial of experienced open-source developers using AI assistance found a 19% productivity slowdown, not a gain. A February 2026 update, addressing a flagged 30-50% task-refusal selection bias in the original cohort, narrowed this to a 4% slowdown (confidence interval -15% to +9%), statistically indistinguishable from zero, with METR now saying AI likely provides a modest benefit in early 2026, well short of any multiple. Source: [METR, "Measuring AI Ability to Complete Long Tasks"](https://metr.org/blog/2025-03-19-measuring-ai-ability-to-complete-long-tasks/); [LessWrong, "Interpreting the METR Time Horizons Post"](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/fRiqwFPiaasKxtJuZ/interpreting-the-metr-time-horizons-post).
- Enterprise ROI benchmarks circulating in the same period put realistic returns at 2.5-3.5x on average and 4-6x for top-quartile organizations, at a reported $200-2,000+ per engineer per month in token spend, explicitly contrasted against vendor-marketing "10x" claims that no independent study has replicated. Source: [Larridin, "Developer Productivity Benchmarks 2026"](https://larridin.com/developer-productivity-hub/developer-productivity-benchmarks-2026); [paddo.dev, "The 10x AI Developer is a Myth"](https://paddo.dev/blog/ai-developer-productivity-myth/).
- Anthropic's own January 2026 research on 52 junior engineers found those using AI assistance scored 17% lower on comprehension tests, with debugging ability hit hardest, a genuine speed-versus-skill-erosion tradeoff that a throughput-only reward mechanic could inadvertently accelerate. Source: cited within [Larridin, "Developer Productivity Benchmarks 2026"](https://larridin.com/developer-productivity-hub/developer-productivity-benchmarks-2026).
- No search in this pass surfaced any external study, dataset, or company replicating a figure anywhere near 16x; the closest published numbers cluster between 1.4x and 6x. This does not mean Carter Razink's number is false; it is his own team's internal claim and this pass could not verify or refute it directly. It does mean the number will land in a room, Allan Marshall included, where the best publicly available reference points sit an order of magnitude lower, which is exactly the tension the product concept overview's own "no overclaim" law (must-have item 20) already exists to police, and exactly why Carter's own 7/15 deliverable (a packaged version with methodology) matters as much as the number itself.

## Options considered

**Option A: ship the league and token-burn mechanic close to the current pitch shape** (a leaderboard-style ranking, token-burn-vs-output score, rewards including comp bumps, in time for the 7/31 Nick spec and a V1 pilot). Fastest to a demo-able mechanic and matches the verbal pitch already tested across the 6/30 and 7/8 calls. But this is structurally very close to Amazon's KiroRank, and the compliance survey above means a comp-linked, formula-driven score is nondiscretionary wage data under the FLSA the moment it touches a non-exempt employee, and a disclosure-and-recordkeeping-triggering automated decision under the EU AI Act, Colorado's SB 26-189, or a German works-council review the moment it "materially influences" pay for a covered employee. Highest adoption-science risk and highest legal risk of the options considered, for the least incremental design work.

**Option B: decouple recognition from binding compensation for V1.** Let the league score drive non-monetary rewards (recognition, time back, cosmetics or points economy, following IBM's BluePoints precedent), and route only aggregated outcome-level movement, not the raw game score, into any real bonus-pool decision, with that decision remaining a human (manager or HR) call rather than an algorithmic payout. This sidesteps most of the FLSA nondiscretionary-formula trap and most of the "materially influences a consequential decision" trigger in the EU and Colorado frameworks, since the system informs rather than determines comp. It also matches the estate's own already-stated law, "measure output and value, not hours or activity," without requiring the outcome-movement KR axis to be fully solved first. Weaker as a standalone sales line than Option A, and still needs real anti-abuse design since even non-monetary recognition triggers the surveillance and demotivation failure modes documented above.

**Option C: skip comp-linkage and leagues entirely for V1.** Ship only the points, streak, and recognition layer already proven in Jarred's own mini achievement engine (single running tally, notable-tier-and-above notifications only, no comp linkage, no competitive per-person ranking) as the enterprise starting point, and treat leagues, anti-abuse, and comp design explicitly as a post-pilot, gated build once Nick's 7/31 spec and a real outcome-data source both exist. Lowest legal exposure of any option: no comp linkage and no per-person competitive ranking means no EU Annex III trigger, no FLSA nondiscretionary-bonus trigger, and no German works-council trigger. Already-shipping code generalizes directly. Weakest as a differentiator, and risks undercutting the "reward not replace" narrative that is core to the pitch, particularly in front of Allan Marshall, who is being sold a $10-50 million vision, not a V1 feature list.

**Option D: treat the compliance design itself as part of the consulting wedge.** Build the "opt-in bonus pool" language already present in the estate's own must-haves list (the $250k-pool example) as a genuinely opt-in, disclosed, and (where a works council exists) works-council-cleared program per client, and sell that design work as a billable line item under the existing "in-the-box software plus out-of-box consultation" model, the same framing already used for the Anchorage-pattern engagement. This turns a real constraint into exactly what the "trust, relationships, speed" moat is supposed to be for, and gives a defensible, non-evasive answer if Anchorage's compliance function or Allan Marshall's counsel asks directly how this avoids an Amazon-KiroRank or Wells-Fargo-style outcome. Adds scope and likely an employment-counsel consult before 7/15 if taken seriously.

## Recommendation with reasoning

Combine Option B and Option D as the actual working design, with Option C as the literal starting point, and hold Option A off the table until a named outcome-data source and at least one client's legal sign-off both exist.

1. **For the 7/15 concept doc and the 7/17-18 Allan Marshall pitch**: keep the vision-level language (leagues, token-burn-vs-output, a rewards economy) largely as is; it is pitching a thesis, not a shipped feature, and the deck's own honest-math discipline already distinguishes vision from built elsewhere. Add one slide or footnote naming the Amazon KiroRank precedent and stating the design principle "reward, but never auto-pay" explicitly. Allan Marshall runs a NASDAQ company and Anchorage is a regulated fintech; a sophisticated buyer in either room is likely to ask some version of "how do you keep this from becoming Amazon's leaderboard," and arriving with the answer already built in is a credibility gain, not a hedge.
2. **For Nick's 7/31 mechanics spec**: make attainment caps with outlier review, role- or cohort-adjusted baselines, and opt-in participation explicit P0 requirements rather than nice-to-haves, citing the Amazon precedent and the Hammedi et al. frontline-service findings as the spec's own evidence base. This is the one gap the product's own scorecard already rates weakest (Str-2) and the one most likely to sink a real deployment if skipped, based on the Amazon case being the closest real-world analog found anywhere in this research pass.
3. **For V1 architecture**: route the score into recognition, time-back, cosmetics, and opt-in bonus-pool eligibility only as a human-reviewed recommendation, never as an automatic payout formula, until the outcome-movement KR axis is real (closing the estate's own self-flagged gap) and at least one pilot client's HR or legal function has signed off on the specific comp mechanism for that client's jurisdiction. This single design choice removes the FLSA nondiscretionary-bonus trap, most of the EU AI Act Annex III trigger, and the Colorado SB 26-189 trigger, while preserving essentially every other selling point in the pitch.
4. **Pressure-test the 16x claim before 7/15**: either have Carter attach a real company name, dataset, and methodology to it, which is already his own promised 7/15 deliverable, or reframe the pitch's headline number toward the loop-deletion and compression framing the State-of-AI deck already uses elsewhere (13 rounds to 1, 24 days to 5, the BCG 40% figure). The best external reference points available right now (METR, enterprise ROI benchmarks) cluster at 1.4x-6x, not 16x, and the gap is large enough that an unqualified 16x claim risks tripping the estate's own "no overclaim" law in front of exactly the audience most likely to know the METR numbers.

## Risks to this recommendation

- Softening comp-linkage for V1 may read as walking back the pitch's most emotionally resonant lines ("an employee who does ten times the work in two hours gets six more hours off in their day," the $250k-pool example) immediately before the highest-stakes pitch of the year. There is a real, legitimate tension between honest-math and legal caution on one side and maximum pitch impact on the other, and Jarred, Nick, and Carter may reasonably choose to accept more risk for pitch impact, especially since nothing is being sold as already shipped.
- The compliance picture above is itself in active flux. The EU AI Act deferral fight was still moving through trilogue as recently as this spring, and Colorado replaced its entire employment-AI framework six weeks before the original law's effective date. Treat this dossier as a dated snapshot (2026-07-08), not a static compliance map, and have counsel re-check anything cited here before it appears in an actual client contract.
- The Amazon KiroRank and Uber/Meta/Microsoft budget-cap stories are drawn from business-press reporting (secondary aggregation of Bloomberg-sourced reporting, plus Blaze Media, IBTimes, and HRD/HCAmag) rather than primary company disclosures. Treat the specific figures (Uber's $500-2,000 per engineer per month, Meta's "billions" warning) as directionally credible but not audited numbers.
- This dossier assumes Anchorage and Upexi/Allan Marshall are the reference cases for near-term compliance exposure. If the actual first pilot lands where the GTM ladder says it will (a C-suite chief-of-staff seat, then an engineering department, both likely salaried and non-exempt-light), several of the sharpest constraints here (FLSA overtime math, EU Annex III, German works councils) may not bind for a year or more, which would mean some of this caution is over-engineered for the actual first deployment and worth re-scoping once a real first client and jurisdiction are named.
- The Carnegie Mellon "moral costs of gamified labor" finding is a normative and philosophical argument, not a business-outcome study. It is real, current academic literature, but it does not by itself predict revenue or retention impact, and should not be weighted above the harder outcome evidence (the Omnisend-versus-Amazon comparison) elsewhere in this dossier.
