Objections rehearsal

Wave-2 meeting pack | Authored 2026-07-09 | Owner: the builder | builder-prep, internal, not sent.

The ten most likely questions, each with a verbal answer the builder can speak in the room. Every answer is five sentences or fewer, concedes the technology baseline plainly, and lands back on the canonical ask. The moat is the organizational psychology of adoption and the trust relationship, never the model.

Canonical ask (character-identical everywhere): A scoped 90-day design-partner pilot plus a smaller strategic check. The larger raise comes at roughly month 6, triggered by demonstrated achievement.

The through-line under every answer: concede capability, pivot to adoption and trust; built things run, designed things are named as designed; the two transactions are separate; no 16x figure, no client names, the product is unnamed by design.

1. "Anthropic or the other labs will just build this."

You're right, and I lead with it: the labs can and will build the capability, that's the market premise, not my fear. What they don't sell is adoption. About 62% of organizations are experimenting and 23% are scaling a single system, with full agentic operation rarer still (McKinsey Nov 2025), and that gap isn't model access, it's that people get penalized for using AI in the open. The moat is the organizational psychology of getting a whole building to actually adopt one system: reward-not-replace, gamified, delivered by a trusted companion. That's exactly what the 90-day pilot proves, which is why capability commoditizing helps me instead of threatening me.

2. "Copilot-class bundles already ship this. Why not just use one?"

A horizontal copilot is raw capability without the organizational container: no objective-scoring spine, no gamified adoption, no earned graduated-autonomy governance. Enterprises don't want more raw capability, they want a safe, governable companion to cross the transition. That container is the product, and it's what the pilot installs and measures against a signed baseline. The copilot in the seat next to it becomes an input to the system, not a competitor to it.

3. "This is a consulting business with thin margins dressed up as software."

I won't pretend it's SaaS margins on day one, because it isn't, and I'd distrust anyone who claimed it was. It's a forward-deployed service becoming software: consulting revenue funds the build and makes the evidence first, and each engagement encodes reusable playbook and product, the way Palantir treats forward-deployed work as product formation. Margin expands only as the reusable-product percentage rises; until then the model reports honest services-band economics with my full delivery cost in it. The pilot P&L I'll show prices my own time fully, so there's no hidden margin. The software margin is named in the future tense, because it's earned, not assumed.

4. "the engineer's team is a lot faster. What's the real multiple?"

That's an internal benchmark from the engineer's engineering framework, and I'll give you the boundary out loud: it's a single-team result, N=1, on a specific task universe, with no baseline normalization yet. So I don't put a multiple on a slide and I don't make it a customer promise. The methodology memo that defines the task universe, the baseline, the comparison design, and the quality measure is a named pilot deliverable, and until it survives an honest-math audit the number stays internal. What I'll stand behind in the room is the engineering lift as a direction, proven on your own definition during the pilot, not a headline figure.

5. "You're a single builder. That's a risk."

It's a fair concern and I treat it as real, not free. I'm the sole builder with the vision and the accountability, and I have two originating members, the game designer on adoption mechanics and the engineer on engineering, earning equity through contribution against named deliverables. The reference implementation isn't tribal knowledge in my head, it's codified in the estate that assigns into the company as background IP, so the operating system survives me. The pilot itself de-risks this: it proves the system runs the work with a human at the gate, which is the opposite of a one-person bottleneck. My builder operating model memo lays out the retained decisions, the delivery capacity, and the named legal and compliance owner.

6. "What happens if you get hit by a bus?"

Honestly, at this stage key-person risk is real, and I'd rather name it than wave it away. Three things soften it: the estate is codified and assigned to the company as background IP, not locked in my head; the originating members can carry their lanes; and the product is a human-gated system that captures decisions and context as it goes, so the operating memory persists. The continuity plan and the named legal and compliance owner live in the builder operating model memo. And the strategic check is sized as runway to a milestone with contingency, not a bet that nothing ever goes wrong.

7. "Why are the game designer and the engineer contributors earning equity, not collaborators?"

Because the vision and the accountability are mine, and I won't paper a three-way founding split that doesn't match reality. They're originating members who earn equity through contribution: a small time-vested base plus milestone kickers tied to named deliverables, each with a written acceptance test. That rewards what they actually deliver, the gamification spec, the engineering framework, the methodology memo, rather than a title granted up front. builder control is preserved as structure, board and voting and reserved decisions, so earned economics never transfer control. It's cleaner for them and for you, because the cap table reflects contribution, not hope.

8. "You're ingesting meetings and OKRs. How is any of this secure?"

Every control I'd show your security team carries an honest maturity label, because nothing designed-but-unbuilt gets described as running. Today the human-gate enforcement is built and tested: nothing touching money, an outside party, or anything irreversible executes without a person deciding. A pilot runs in a dedicated, isolated workspace walled from my estate and from other clients, with documented consent, all-party consent on recordings, and stated retention and deletion timelines, and those are prerequisites before day-one capture. I never imply pooled client-data learning; what compounds is client-specific configuration, not shared data. The full data-flow, access, retention, and incident-response annex is the artifact your security lead reviews before any production data moves.

9. "Why doesn't the product have a name?"

Intentionally, and I own it as a strength. The product is intentionally unnamed; I'll choose the permanent mark from a legally cleared shortlist after the position is locked. For today the working descriptor is agent OS for organizations, and the decision in front of us is the 90-day design partnership, not a wordmark. A counsel-cleared naming process runs on its own track, and the entity forms this week under a plain legal name, because naming before clearance is just buying a collision.

10. "What do you actually need me to decide today?"

Two separate decisions, and only two. First, whether to scope a 90-day design-partner pilot; that's your operator seat, and it lives on the one-page charter. Second, and separately, whether to consider a smaller strategic check; that's your owner seat, papered on its own with no contingency to the pilot. The larger raise isn't on the table today; it comes at roughly month 6, triggered by demonstrated achievement. A yes to one is a real yes and is never a yes to both, so you can take them one at a time.

The honesty rule (when you don't know)

If a question lands outside what the evidence supports, say so plainly and name it as a pilot deliverable or an open item the builder owns. "I don't have a defensible number for that yet, and here's when I will" outperforms a confident guess in this room. Radical honesty about capabilities is itself the moat (grok, round 2); a single caught overclaim costs the trust thesis permanently.
