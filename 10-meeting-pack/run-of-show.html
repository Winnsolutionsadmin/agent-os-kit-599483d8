Governing law: repo CLAUDE.md corrections; ADDENDUM 4 (R1-R8) > ADDENDUM 3 (S1-S4, B1-B11) > earlier addendums. The deck it choreographs around is 09-deliverables/pitch-deck/source.md. Hard constraints baked in: no em dashes; the canonical ask is character-identical to the deck; nothing designed-but-unbuilt speaks in the present tense (B built things run, designed things are named as designed); no 16x figure anywhere (R4); no client names (gate C); the product is unnamed by design (R1, S3, S4); the two transactions are never blended (B1).

The single design goal (kimi, round 3): engineer the whole window for one conversion event. Best outcome is a signed pilot plus a separate diligence path; the acceptable outcome is a sponsor yes plus a scoping session scheduled within 72 hours. Enthusiasm without named owners and dates is not an achievement.

Pre-meeting checklist (done before the room, not in it)

Nothing below is optional. Two items block the meeting outright (R8 charter; demo controls).

  • [ ] The one-page pilot charter is drafted with the named fields as blanks-to-fill (operating counterparty, executive sponsor, workflow owner, data owner, baseline, success threshold, kill criterion, fee, cadence, day-90 decision). Source: 09-deliverables/legal-readiness/source.md document 4. This document BLOCKS 7/17.
  • [ ] Both branches rehearsed (counterparty branch and sponsor branch; see the two-branch close below).

  • [ ] Do NOT send the reading room as homework. It exists, and it is offered on request only.
  • [ ] Ask the investor, in the same send, what they most want to see in the room (this pre-loads the demo question choice).

  • [ ] Timestamped recording and screenshots captured from the same data as the honest fallback, and the fallback is tested.
  • [ ] Five clean rehearsals logged, no write-capable credentials present, before July 16.

  • [ ] An investment-diligence calendar is open and ready to book (for the strategic-check path).

5. Scrub the materials (remove before rehearsal)

  • [ ] No slide implies an executed agreement (B10); the fleet-of-one is labeled a reference implementation everywhere (gate D).

Verbatim cards (say these word for word)

THE CANONICAL ASK (character-identical everywhere; never re-parametrized):

A scoped 90-day design-partner pilot plus a smaller strategic check. The larger raise comes at roughly month 6, triggered by demonstrated achievement.

THE UNNAMED-STATUS SCRIPT (R1, S3, S4; spoken at the open, own it as strength):

The product is intentionally unnamed. I'll choose the permanent mark from a legally cleared shortlist after the position is locked. For today, the working descriptor is agent OS for organizations. The decision in front of us is the 90-day design partnership, not a wordmark.

Never say "brand in stealth." Never present "agent OS" as a name; it is a lowercase descriptor, used sparingly.

THE DEMO OPENING LINE (spoken as the readout comes up):

This reads my estate right now. It cannot write.

THE READ-ONLY GUARDRAIL (G1, claude; spoken at demo start, right after the opening line):

What you are seeing is read-only: the system observed and assembled this from connected sources on request, it took no action, wrote nothing, and decided nothing; the relay work behind it is delivery plumbing we are proving, not autonomy we are claiming, and you will hear it from us the moment that line moves.

THE AUDIT-RECEIPT PRECISION LINE (G2, codex; spoken when the audit receipt is shown):

For precision, READ ONLY means no source-system mutation and no external action; the only write is an idempotent append to the local audit log, and it does not send, approve, or continue an agent loop.

THE THREE PRE-CLEARED DEMO QUESTIONS (investor picks one; all three cleared under R3):

  1. What needs my attention right now?
  2. What changed since yesterday?
  3. How does current work map to one objective?

THE DEMO CLOSING QUESTION (asked after the readout, hands the room over):

What would this need to see, and refuse to do, before you would trust it inside an organization?

THE DEMO-FAILURE FALLBACK LINE (if the live readout fails, say it plainly, do not improvise a save):

That is not loading the way it does in rehearsal, so I am not going to talk you through a screen that is misbehaving. Here is the timestamped recording of the same readout, and I will narrate exactly what you are seeing.

Then switch to the recording. Never call a static illustrative page live (B2). A failed live path handled honestly costs nothing; a faked one costs the room permanently.

The 60 minutes

Timings are targets, not a script to race. The one non-negotiable rhythm (R5): ask early as the frame, one live trace in the middle, dated next step re-landed at the close.

0 to 4 min: the ask, as orientation

Goal: the investor reads everything after this through the ask.

  • Open on the cover. Deliver the unnamed-status script verbatim (card above). Do not linger; the name is a settled non-issue, owned in one breath.
  • Advance to the ask slide and state the canonical ask verbatim. Frame it as two decisions: "I am asking for two separate decisions." The pilot is the operator seat; the check is the owner seat; papered separately, never blended.
  • Say plainly that a yes to one is never a yes to both, and that the larger raise is later and is earned by demonstrated achievement, not asked for today.

Guardrail: no instrument, no check size, no fee spoken here or anywhere on a slide. The ask is the frame, not the negotiation.

4 to 10 min: the thesis (premise, not gamification)

Goal: establish that the unresolved layer is adoption and trust, not raw capability.

  • Concede capability plainly: the frontier labs can and will build agent capability. That is the market premise, not our fear.
  • State the real blocker: capability is arriving, adoption is not. Use the sourced numbers only (SURVEY, McKinsey Nov 2025): 62% of organizations are experimenting, 23% are scaling a single system, and full agentic operation is rarer still; plus the disclosure penalty (workers rated lazier and less promotable at identical output when they admit using AI). The gap is human, so the fix is organizational psychology.
  • Name the wedge and why this builder: a C-suite that finally has a chief of staff which never sleeps, then engineering wants the overhaul, then the rest is pulled in by demand; and the builder runs the reference implementation today, at fleet-of-one scale.

Guardrail: do NOT lead with gamification. It is the adoption engine, introduced later as pilot-scope, never the opening pitch. Fleet-of-one is a reference implementation, never an installed customer (gate D).

10 to 15 min: the truth table and at most three estate proofs

Goal: an uninvolved reviewer can classify every visible capability without asking the builder.

  • Put up the four-column truth table (B4): operating internally, prototype, pilot build, horizon. Every capability sits in exactly one column. Built things run; designed things are named as designed.
  • Present at most THREE proofs, each answering "what changed for the buyer, or what risk did the system control" (B3). Lead with the one reproducible trace (shown live in the next block). The two supporting MEASURED proofs: the OKR engine scored 99.3% of commits against the objective tree on its first run (275 of 277), and one operator cleared 39 overdue follow-ups and recovered 3 to 6 hours a week.
  • Say once, out loud, that all raw activity counts (skills, commits, points, streaks) live in the appendix, labeled N=1 operating evidence, because they prove sustained builder use, not customer traction (R6, gate E).

Guardrail: three proofs maximum in this block. Do not tour the seven pillars; that is the named failure mode (a polished platform tour instead of one pilot decision).

15 to 25 min: the read-only demo (the central evidence event)

Goal: show trust as product behavior, not brand language. One sourced estate readout, live, READ ONLY.

  1. Open with the boundary, before anything moves: say the demo opening line ("This reads my estate right now. It cannot write."), then deliver the read-only guardrail G1 verbatim (card above). The READ ONLY label is already up and stays up.
  2. Hand the investor the choice. Offer the three pre-cleared questions and let them pick one:
  • What needs my attention right now?
  • What changed since yesterday?
  • How does current work map to one objective?
  1. Run the readout on their choice. Narrate only the data path actually observed: the source event, its provenance, the freshness timestamp. Show the related objective and the prior context the system retrieved.
  2. Show the uncertainty or refusal moment on purpose (R3). Surface the one field the system flags low-confidence and says it is not sure of, or the action it will not take because that is outside its authority. This moment is the point of the demo, not a blemish: the system shows what it does not know instead of hiding it.
  3. State the boundary again as you land: it detected, retrieved, rendered, and appended to an append-only receipt. As the audit receipt is shown, deliver the precision line G2 verbatim (card above). It did not send anything and did not act outside the gate. Do not narrate closed-loop relay; the spike detects, renders, appends, and stays idempotent, and does not yet consume its own answer and continue (B2). When it does, the investor will watch it, not hear it claimed.
  4. Hand the room over with the closing question: "What would this need to see, and refuse to do, before you would trust it inside an organization?" Then stop talking and let them answer; their answer is the pilot's real acceptance criteria, in their own words.

If it fails: deliver the demo-failure fallback line verbatim (card above), switch to the timestamped recording, narrate the same trace. Do not improvise a live save.

Guardrail: no writes, no sends, no action-shaped buttons, ever, even if the investor asks to "just try it." Faking interactivity here is the single room-losing mistake all models converged on (see DO-NOTs below).

25 to 33 min: the narrow product loop

Goal: show the product as one repeatable loop, not a platform inventory.

  • Walk the loop once: context in, one sourced recommendation out, the human approves, edits, or skips, the decision is logged to an append-only receipt, and authority expands only by explicit evidence.
  • State the autonomy law as risk-reduction structure, not compliance overhead: nothing that touches money, an outside party, or anything irreversible moves without a person deciding. An advisor acts on a decision class on its own only after it has matched the human's actual choice at least 90% of the time across at least 20 reps. No tier buys an exception.
  • Frame the pilot product in one sentence (the codex product line): during the pilot, the product turns one organization's meetings, objectives, and workflow state into sourced recommendations an executive can approve, edit, or skip.

Guardrail: the system orchestrates; humans execute the final gate. Never say it "runs your company." Graduated autonomy is earned in reps, never sold in tiers.

33 to 43 min: the 90-day pilot (30/60/90)

Goal: the investor sees a costed, phased, falsifiable engagement, not a promise.

  • Days 1 to 30 (present-safe: this is the pilot build, stated as the plan): set data boundaries and record baselines; capture goes live read-only on a designated, disclosed surface; one gated workflow, chosen with the partner in week one, goes into production behind the Approve / Edit / Skip gate. Zero ungated actions. The partner signs a two-week time baseline and their day-zero follow-up debt first. Day-30 gate.
  • Days 31 to 60: the system produces measured recommendations against the baseline; every action stays behind an instrumented gate; rework, latency, and safety are tracked. Day-60 gate.
  • Days 61 to 90: the opt-in adoption cohort enters, anti-abuse on from its first day; adoption is measured. The evidence holds or it does not: hours recovered, follow-up debt near zero, at least one decision class at or past graduation or an honest reason why not. Day-90 gate, and the stop / extend / expand decision.

Guardrail: every threshold number (capture coverage, gated-execution count, adoption rate, hours recovered) is a candidate until the partner signs the baseline; keep the numbers in discovery, not on the slide. Before day 1 there is a signed baseline and a written kill criterion. Gamification is pilot-scope, named in the future tense, never described as running today.

43 to 49 min: economics and capital (two transactions, kept apart)

Goal: the shape of the economics, and what the check does and does not fund, with the two transactions visibly separate.

  • The economics as a shape: consulting first, software margin layered later; the delivery is a forward-deployed service becoming software (the Palantir shape, cited as the shape, never as our current margin). Priced against a job you would otherwise hire (a chief of staff plus an engineering lift), not a per-seat license.
  • The pilot half: the 90-day pilot is a costed engagement with the builder's own delivery time fully priced in, deliberately concessionary for a design partner. Give the investor's own capital logic: hours recovered at his own rate, measured against the baseline he signs, plus a production workflow he keeps.
  • The capital half, kept separate: the strategic check is sized as runway to the roughly month-6 achievement milestone plus contingency, less pilot revenue that offsets the same period. Say what the check does not fund and what achievements are required before the larger raise. It funds reaching the milestone; it does not price the company.

Guardrail: no fee, no check size, no cap, no instrument, no multiplier spoken (R4, S1). The pilot fee is not tied to any check and no check is conditioned on the pilot (B1). If the check is not ready for counsel-cleared discussion, ask for the pilot and book the investment-diligence session instead of improvising terms.

49 to 54 min: the team and the legal state, honestly

Goal: the investor can accept the team facts without discovering a contradiction later.

  • the builder is the sole builder; the vision is his; he operates the reference implementation. the game-design collaborator and the engineering collaborator are originating members earning equity through contribution, deliverable-tied and vested. Not a three-way split, no collaborators framing.
  • State the legal state plainly: entity formation and IP assignment are in flight this week; the interim facts memorandum targets 7/11. Anything not signed is named as pending with a date. Nothing here implies an agreement that is not signed (B10).
  • Preserve builder control as structure: board composition, voting, and reserved decisions, not just equity percentage. the engineer's the prior engineering framework framework is his property and an input to the engineering pillar, not the company's product.

Guardrail: no slide or sentence implies executed contributor grants or assigned IP that are not yet signed. Consulting clients are consulting clients, never product customers; the signed pilot is the first product-market achievement.

54 to 60 min: turn the room over, then the dated close

Goal: leave with named owners and dates for both transactions, or an explicit no.

  • Turn the room over: ask what evidence would justify day-90 expansion, and what evidence would justify the month-6 raise. Their answers become the written success criteria, not yours.
  • Re-land the canonical ask verbatim (card above), then name the concrete dated next step within a week, using the correct branch below.
  • Close on named people and dates: a pilot-scoping session and an investment-diligence session, booked as two separate things. A yes to one is not represented as a yes to both.

Guardrail: do not leave without stating the singular ask; that is the third converged room-losing mistake. Book the calendar in the room; "we'll find time" is not a close.

The two-branch close (R8; rehearse both before 7/17)

Which branch is primary is decided in the pre-meeting checklist, based on the role the investor plays. Rehearse both; the room may reveal the role only late.

Branch A: the investor IS the operating counterparty

  • The one-page pilot charter goes on the table, fields ready to fill: named operating counterparty, executive sponsor, workflow owner, data owner, baseline, success threshold, kill criterion, fee, cadence, day-90 decision.
  • The dated next step is a pilot-scoping session to fill the remaining blanks and name the one gated workflow, plus a separate investment-diligence session if the check is live.
  • A fully signed pilot in the first meeting is not required and not expected; a filled charter and a booked scoping session is a full success.

Branch B: the investor is the sponsor, not the operator

  • The charter does not get signed in the room. The ask becomes sponsorship plus the strategic check plus a dated scoping session, target within 72 hours, to name the operating counterparty.
  • Concrete close: "Who should run this, and can we get 30 minutes with them inside 72 hours?" Book that session before leaving.
  • After the scoping session, the same charter goes to the named counterparty.

Branch C: a graceful pass (state it as acceptable)

  • A pass keeps the relationship. The line: we come back with a signed partner's day-30 evidence, not a re-pitch.
  • This reads as "stay in your lane" respect and keeps the relationship warm for the achievement-triggered raise later.

The three room-losing mistakes (DO-NOTs; all models converged)

DO NOT fake interactivity in the read-only demo. State the boundary proudly and keep it. If a click, animation, narration, or transcript append implies the system took an action or resumed a live agent when it did not, the trusted-companion thesis collapses and every later proof becomes suspect. If the live path breaks, use the fallback line and the recording; never pretend.

DO NOT let the investor re-scope the terms live. If they start redrawing the pilot or the check in the room, listen fully, then return to the written scope: "Let me capture that and bring it back in the scoping session." Propose a follow-up for the deltas. Do not renegotiate the charter or the check on the fly.

DO NOT leave without stating the singular ask. Close by restating the canonical ask verbatim and booking the dated next step. Warmth without a named owner and a date on the calendar is not an achievement.
