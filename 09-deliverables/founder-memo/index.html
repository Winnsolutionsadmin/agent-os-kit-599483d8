a company being named, on purpose

[ The Memo ] · winn.solutions · July 9, 2026 · [ Draft ]

A note on what this is. This memo is the conviction behind the company, written in first person on purpose. If you want the numbers first, start with the financials and market case and the 16× case, shown, then come back here for the why.

My voice, my draft, mine to cut.

Every kind of work just became improvable.

Literally all of my work runs through agents now. Every piece of it. Code, legal, ops, design, outreach. Each one got better the day an agent started carrying it, and I have watched that hold in my own shop every single day for months. That observation is the whole origin of this thing, so let me sit on it.

Over the last few months my entire operation moved inside a fleet of agents that runs for exactly one person: me. There’s an engine that scores the work against goals I actually set, a layer that makes the doing of it feel like progress instead of grind, a pipeline that turns meetings into tracked action instead of forgotten talk, and a few hundred skills I’ve built up one problem at a time. It carries the things I’m weak at, the language, the code, the technical gaps, so I get to spend myself only on the parts where I’m worth ten times more. It runs my consulting, my other companies, and most of my day. It’s not something I spin up for a demo. It’s what I use to live. My first instinct with anyone I respect is to turn the screen around and show them what’s already possible.

That instinct is the company. Not the model underneath it. The lived proof that one person, with the right companion, can operate like a team. A company gets built on what its owner actually believes, and this one is built on mine.

Why I can’t leave this alone.

I need to be honest about the pull here, because it did not start as a market analysis. What I am holding feels like a cheat code. One person with this stack operates like a team, and it took me months, not years, to get there. The people who pick these tools up first are going to compound wealth and leverage so fast it splits society into classes, and I got handed an early copy. Sitting on a cheat code and quietly getting rich with it is the move I watch everyone else make, and I think it is the wrong end of the entire thing.

Almost everyone points AI at making money. The people who matter in this era will point it at other people. Let the machines carry the work so the humans can spend themselves on judgment, on ideas, on each other. That is what my own fleet actually gave me back: hours with my kids, attention for the people I love, work that fits in one window instead of swallowing the whole day. I know what that feels like from the inside now, and I cannot know it and then watch companies roll AI out as a threat memo and a layoff plan. Somebody has to carry organizations through this transition without flattening the people inside them. I have the working proof in my hands. That is the compulsion.

The enemy isn’t AI. It’s how AI is landing on people.

Let me say the quiet part plainly. Most companies are adopting AI in a way that scares their people and flattens them at the same time. Fear-based mandates from the top. Replace-the-headcount math in the boardroom. And underneath it, something subtler that bothers me more: everyone starts prompting the same way, reaching for the same outputs, and slowly sounding like each other.

I want you to maintain your unique sense of individuality. As AI is adopted and we’re all influenced by it, we lose our unique sense of individuality and humanity. I believe that genuinely enough that I’m writing a book about it. The more we all lean on the same models, the more we converge, and the part that gets quietly deleted is the part of a person that was actually theirs. A world governed entirely by mathematical optimization is a boring, sad world. Perfection is boring. Chaotic, ungoverned adoption doesn’t just burn people out. It homogenizes them.

And the deepest cut isn’t the jobs. It’s the authenticity. When AI writes and creates and decides on our behalf, everything turns into mathematical output, and the scarce thing becomes the human hand behind the work. Soon we won’t see Made in China. We’ll see Made by Human. That’s the enemy. Not the technology. The careless way it’s being poured into organizations.

Here is where it stopped being abstract for me. I watched two of my sons play rock-paper-scissors, and one of them won every single time. Every time. I finally asked the winner how he did it, and he had cracked his brother’s pattern: he throws rock first, and after that he always gives paper. So I pulled the other one aside, taught him the counters, and the games started going the other way. Then it hit me. Was that parenting, or was that an algorithm? For all of history a father handed his kids his own hard-won edge at exactly that kind of moment. We are right at the point where a machine can hand it to them instead. I keep landing on the same question: will my kids remember the moment their father taught them something, or will mathematical probability be their teacher?

So with my own kids I do the opposite on purpose. I teach my boys to use these tools next to me, at the table. We’re building a Roblox game together this summer, them and me and Claude, because the answer was never to keep the tool away from them. It’s to be in the room while they learn it, so it stays a tool and never becomes their teacher. The book I’m writing has a four-word thesis: stay human, enjoy moments. That is the same thing I want for every person inside a company that runs this.

So the pitch is the opposite of what everyone else is selling.

We’re not doing this so you can replace your employees. We’re doing this so you can reward your employees and retain them, based on the fact that they are then going to be adopting AI. This was never mainly a labor-replacement story. It’s a migration of advantage toward the people who actually restructure around the tool, and I’d rather that be your people than someone else’s. If a person does ten times the work in two hours, the answer isn’t to fire the other nine. It’s to give that person their afternoon back. You buy people time instead of taking their jobs.

And it isn’t only software. It’s not only a software suite for your team, but also a consultative process of ensuring that your company is running in the right ways, which is where the human element comes into it. That consulting isn’t a bolt-on. It’s the bridge, and it’s the point.

Why a companion, and not just a smarter model.

Let me concede the thing most owners in this space tiptoe around. Anthropic and the others can build this. The raw capability isn’t my moat, and pretending otherwise would insult anyone smart enough to write me a check. We know they can do this. What companies need is a trusted companion to bring them through it and help them get a head start. The hard part was never the intelligence or the capability. It’s whether the interaction feels human, warm, and trustworthy. A model is a tool. A companion is a relationship, and the relationship is the whole game.

Trust is a factor of time, consistency, and the experiences you share along the way. It’s representative of value transference accumulated through action and sacrifice. You can’t download that. It’s why the labs, for all their capability, aren’t going to sit at a nervous executive’s side for six months and earn the right to change how their company runs. I already have. That’s the moat: the organizational psychology of getting real people to adopt this without losing themselves, and the trust built one shared experience at a time.

The order it spreads is deliberate. C-levels first, the chief of staff and the organizer, the people drowning in coordination. Engineers next, to revamp and overhaul how the work gets built. Everyone else follows once they see it, on demand rather than by mandate. The industry argues endlessly about governing AI. Almost nobody is working on the other side of it, how a person governs their own use so they stay themselves, and that’s the part I care about most. So adoption here is something people opt into, never something done to them.

How this gets funded.

I’m not running a cold process. We raise on achievement and faith, from investors we already know. People who’ve watched me do things and believe I’ll do this one. Capital is itself a demonstration of trust. It doesn’t move without one, so the first check comes from someone I’ve already made money before, not a stranger running an audit. The near-term ask is small and specific: a scoped 90-day design-partner pilot plus a smaller strategic check, sized to what a live demonstration actually earns. The larger raise comes later, at roughly month six, triggered by demonstrated achievement and nothing else. I’d rather show you the thing running than sell you a slide about it.

On structure, so it is never fuzzy: I am the owner of this. The vision is mine, the reference implementation is mine, and the accountability is mine. Help, when I engage it, earns in through real contribution recorded in writing. Nobody gets a title for showing up.

Where this goes, grounded in what already runs.

Ten years out, I think every serious organization runs on one window of consequence. Not seventeen terminal windows. Not a notification feed. Not a chatbot. One surface that shows you what you’re working on and asks you only about the decisions that genuinely need a human, while the routine triage gets handled underneath and always stops at your approval. The AI guides and never instructs, and it stays a tool, never an identity. People spend their time being most creative, because the communication gaps and the busywork are finally solved. That future is a bet, and I’m stating it as a bet. But it’s not a fantasy, because a narrow version of it already runs today, for one person, and I’m that person.

Which brings me back to the name, or the lack of one. It sits in brackets right now, [ PROJ-X ], and I’m keeping it there until it’s earned. Your name is your brand, and public actions are unscrubbable, so every present action is a building block. In a category whose whole sickness is rushing to look like everyone else, I’m not going to name my company before I know exactly what it is. Being unnamed isn’t a gap. It’s the first small act of the thing I’m actually building.

What would make this wrong.

I want this kit to be falsifiable, so here is the honest kill-list. If frontier models get so good that a bare chat window closes the adoption gap on its own, with no companion layer, no mechanics, and no memory, the wedge narrows. If organizations turn out to reject ambient agents no matter how they are introduced, the thesis dies at the psychology layer, not the technology layer. And if what works for one operator running one estate refuses to transfer to a second organization, then this is a story about me and not a company. The first pilot exists to test exactly that third risk, on purpose, before any bigger promise is made.

What this is not.

It is not a wrapper that resells someone else’s model with a nicer prompt. It is not another copilot bolted into a sidebar, waiting to be ignored. It is not consulting dressed up as software. The services lane funds the build; it is not the product. And it is not married to one model vendor: the estate that runs today swaps engines underneath without the operating layer noticing. What compounds here is not the model. It is the accumulated skills, memory, and judgment sitting on top of whichever model is best this quarter.

The purpose of life is other people. It all comes down to the relationships that you have. Almost everyone points AI at making money, which is the wrong end of it. The people who win this era are those who use AI to connect and build with belief-aligned humans. I’m building a machine that gives people more room for that, not less. That’s the entire reason I’m doing this. Everything above is just the plan.
