## 1. Top 10 improvements, ranked by leverage

1. **Lock one canonical "ask" and make every artifact deliver exactly it.** The single organizing risk this launch carries is evidence-versus-ask mismatch. The whole kit must be built to earn a scoped 90-day design-partner pilot plus a smaller strategic check, with the larger syndicate raise named explicitly as a later, achievement-triggered step. Write the ask once, in one sentence, and reuse it verbatim in the deck, the one-pager, the concept overview, and the term-sheet draft. A kit whose mouth says "pilot" while the numbers imply a big round reads as incoherent to someone who already knows Jarred, and incoherence is fatal to conviction capital. This is the cheapest high-leverage fix in the set.

2. **Package one live, watch-it-happen front-door demo from the estate.** The panel's stated highest-leverage move is turning the fleet-of-one estate into a demonstrable pilot. Do the exec-agent-gui answer-relay spike so there is ONE real front door the investor can watch triage a real decision and relay a real approval, not only the four published mocks. "This operation is the demonstration" is the only advantage no competitor can copy this quarter. Make it literally visible in the room.

3. **Harden or demote the 16x case study before it touches any external surface, and prebuild the fallback now.** This is the most load-bearing and most diligence-fragile claim in the whole story. Methodology memo is due 7/15; if it lands and is defensible, use it with full tag lineage. If it slips or is thin, the deck must already contain the output-parity framing ("4 matched the output of 18") as a clean drop-in that needs no restructuring. Do not let the kit be hostage to a 7/15 delivery you do not control.

4. **Build the unit-economics / ICP / buyer-ROI model as the spine of the kit, not an appendix.** This is blind spot number one, named by three to four models, and it does not exist yet. Without who-pays, which budget line, the baseline pain metric, 30/60/90 success thresholds, expansion math, and the consulting-versus-software margin mix, the pilot ask has no falsifiable value claim and the room has nothing to underwrite. activeContext already says fill this first; that instinct is correct.

5. **Stand up the entity, IP assignment, and contribution-earns-equity framework THIS WEEK.** Counsel called a missing entity survival-critical and a fatal flaw. No artifact should promise equity or take a check into a non-entity, and the reference-implementation IP must be cleanly assignable out of Jarred's multi-purpose personal estate (which currently entangles client work and out-of-scope ventures). This unblocks the ability to actually accept even a small strategic check.

6. **Write a one-page data-governance and tenant-isolation posture before the deck ships.** Blind spot number two, and the first question any design partner's security team asks. Meeting, OKR, and scoring data is surveillance-shaped; you need a stated stance on consent, retention, segregation, breach response, and surveillance risk. In 2026 governance is the product story; absence of a posture reads as naivety to anyone technical.

7. **Write the founder operating model plus a capacity plan for the collision window.** Blind spot number three. Jarred is a single operator running client delivery, the raise, and the build at once, with a live calendar collision (client sprint work overlapping the 7/11 doc, 7/15 deck, and 7/17-18 meeting). A sophisticated reader will ask how one person sustains this. Name the decision rights delegated to Nick and Carter, the single-operator capacity plan, and a named legal/compliance owner.

8. **Resolve the gamification story into a crisp, defensible anti-abuse posture now, ahead of Nick's 7/31 spec.** Reward-not-replace is the emotional core and the most misread pillar (real backlash risk). The kit needs one tight paragraph: caps, outlier detection, role-adjusted scoring, decoupled from comp during the pilot, measure output not hours. Do not describe leagues and economies you have not built as if they exist; keep the built-versus-designed line clean.

9. **Install a shared claims ledger and honest-math QA gate across every artifact.** The MEASURED / SURVEY / VENDOR / [unverified] discipline is the credibility moat, and it matters more here because the recipient explicitly prefers honesty. Source each stat once into a shared ledger and have every deliverable pull from it, so no artifact re-invents a number. One overclaim in one document taints the whole set.

10. **Sweep relationship open-loops and lock the written dual-role disclosure before the meeting.** Walking into a known-investor ask with visible open loops on the same relationship is a self-inflicted diligence flag. Put any separate paid or advisory relationship into an ordinary written conflict-of-interest disclosure before money moves, routed through counsel with the securities pass. Cheap hygiene that protects the exact relationship the funding model depends on.

## 2. Per deliverable: 3 to 5 concrete musts, plus the single most common failure mode

### (a) Pitch deck for known-investor conviction pitches
- Open on the relationship and the trusted-companion category, not a cold problem slide; lead with why this founder, why now, why a head start, because this is conviction capital from someone who knows Jarred.
- One explicit "The Ask" slide: scoped 90-day design-partner pilot plus a smaller strategic check, with the larger raise named as a later achievement-triggered step. No large raise number anywhere.
- Put the estate-is-the-demonstration proof front and center: the live front-door demo (or an embedded recording), plus the fleet HUD, OKR engine, and live achievement streak as evidence it already runs.
- Build the 16x slide to swap cleanly between hardened-methodology and output-parity versions; tags visible; no raw multiplier as a headline.
- Name competitors and the reward-not-replace thesis head-on; acknowledge frontier-lab capability as the market premise, per decision 0003.
- **Most common failure mode:** the deck quietly inflates into a big-raise institutional pitch, breaking the achievement-plus-faith framing and reading as incoherent to someone who knows the true stage. (Corollary: a readiness score sneaks onto a slide.)

### (b) Brand package, naming CANDIDATES only
- Present 3 to 5 named territories (for example: calm-companion, operating-surface, trust-and-attestation, orchestration/cartography), each with 2 to 3 candidates, a rationale, and the feeling it evokes; never rank one as the pick.
- Run a same-day knockout screen on every survivor: trademark exact and phonetic in classes 9 and 42, live .com and .ai domains, GitHub/npm/PyPI org, social handles, existing-AI-company collision. Present the screen results with the names.
- Explicitly retire the ruled-out names in the doc (FleetView, TeamClaude GUI, gui-relay, mission control) and restate that Recurro is Carter's own framework, off the table; and that "Agent OS" is a descriptor, not a candidate.
- Apply trust-positioning constraints: avoid names that overclaim autonomy or omniscience, avoid surveillance registers, favor calm/partnership/human-agency, 2 to 3 syllables, spellable on first hearing.
- **Most common failure mode:** presenting a single favorite dressed up as "options," which usurps the founder's explicit decision right, and burning the screen on names that turn out legally dead.

### (c) Whitepaper
- Lead with the trusted-companion thesis and the human-transition arc, then architecture, then gamification, then the human-gate and graduated-autonomy law (90% agreement over 20-plus reps per decision class) as a named governing principle.
- Draw a hard line between what is BUILT (fleet-of-one: HUD, OKR engine, achievement engine, capture pipeline, 296-skill registry) and what is DESIGNED (per-person gamification, multi-tenancy, attribution). Never let roadmap speak in the present tense.
- Every quantitative claim carries its source and tag; frame productivity honestly (task-level 20 to 40 percent controlled, firm-level 5 to 15 percent, loop deletion over multipliers).
- State the safety laws plainly: no auto-action on money, irreversible, or external actions without a human gate; fail open to surfacing; untrusted-content discipline; reward-not-replace.
- **Most common failure mode:** it reads as the spec of a finished multi-tenant product rather than a thesis plus a reference implementation, so the built-versus-designed line blurs and the document overclaims.

### (d) Digestible 2 to 3 page executive concept overview
- Ruthlessly compress: what it is, why now, the seven pillars in one line each, the domino GTM, the ask. Nothing that needs a footnote.
- Written for a C-level reader in five minutes, one strong visual (the front door or the domino ladder), plain language, no jargon.
- Carries the same canonical ask and the same honest-math discipline as the deck, and survives being forwarded without Jarred in the room.
- End on a concrete next step (the pilot shape), not a vision flourish.
- **Most common failure mode:** it becomes a compressed everything and loses the single takeaway, so the reader finishes unable to say in one sentence what the company is.

### (e) One-pager
- Truly one page: category headline, the transition/problem, a three-to-five-word product description, the proof (the estate already runs it), the ask, and contact.
- Include one memorable category sentence a reader can repeat verbatim ("the trusted companion that brings companies through the AI transition").
- Belief-indexed if it goes to a named recipient; honest-but-generic if it is a leave-behind.
- Stylized HTML or PDF, never raw markdown.
- **Most common failure mode:** cramming the whole deck onto one page in tiny type; the one-pager must trade completeness for a single clean hook.

### (f) Founder vision memo in the founder voice
- First person, in Jarred's real cadence; anchor on his verbatim north-star lines (compression of time between thought and output; humanity preservation; reward employees rather than replace them).
- Make the structure explicit and generous: the vision is Jarred's, Nick and Carter earn equity through contribution, no three-founders framing.
- Serve as the emotional spine: why this, why him, why now, and what he refuses to build (no undisclosed capture, no replace-the-human).
- Be honest about stage: this is conviction, not a finished product; he is asking people who know him to bet on demonstrated achievement.
- **Most common failure mode:** it drifts into third-person marketing polish and loses the founder voice, becoming interchangeable with any AI-startup manifesto. Run it through a voice and anti-AI-writing pass.

### (g) Financial model scaffold + ICP + buyer ROI
- Name the ICP precisely: the first domino is the C-level chief-of-staff seat; give firmographics, the budget line the money comes from, and the buyer's baseline pain metric.
- Make buyer ROI falsifiable: baseline captured before deployment, a defensible 20 to 40 percent workflow-time-reduction range (not a multiplier), 30/60/90 thresholds, and a stated kill criterion.
- Model the consulting-versus-software margin mix and the cash-plus-equity structure honestly; show the pilot as a near-free first-rung loss-leader kept separate from any later capital ask.
- Give category pricing reference points (enterprise assistant seats run in the tens of dollars per user per month, large annual deployments in the low-to-mid six figures) with the honest note that no vendor sells exactly this yet.
- Tag every number as assumption versus sourced; the model is a scaffold with visible levers, not false precision.
- **Most common failure mode:** false precision, a hockey-stick built on invented conversion rates and no baseline, which destroys credibility faster than shipping no model at all.

### (h) Reading-room index
- A curated, tiered index (start-here, then depth) with a one-line "what this is and why read it" per entry, pointing to the concept overview, whitepaper, demos, and evidence.
- Gate shareability: wall off internal-only material (readiness scores, blunt competitive-risk dossiers, the unhardened case study) from the investor-facing room.
- Link the live published demos prominently (the strongest visual proof) and verify each link is live before sharing.
- Make it navigable in under a minute; the index itself should signal rigor.
- **Most common failure mode:** it leaks internal-only material into the shared room, or ships with dead links that quietly contradict the "we already operate this" claim.

### (i) Evolution of the State of AI teaching deck (keep it education, not a pitch)
- Hold the red lines absolute: no pitch, no collaborator names, belief-indexed to the recipient, honest sourced math. The trusted-companion arc and domino ladder enter as EDUCATION about the market transition, never as a product ask.
- Add the arc as a "where this is heading" narrative (companies will need a companion to cross the transition; adoption ladders from C-suite to engineering to the wider org), framed generically, not as "our company does this."
- Run the belief-profile preflight on the named recipient before authoring (the "light, lame, bland" lesson); recipient-indexed or it fails.
- Preserve the existing honest-math tags and loop-deletion economics; import no unhardened claim (no raw 16x).
- Keep it a physically separate artifact from the pitch deck so the two never cross-contaminate.
- **Most common failure mode:** the education deck quietly becomes a pitch (a product logo, an ask, or a collaborator name creeps in), violating the red lines and burning trust with a recipient who was promised education.

### (j) Legal-readiness DRAFTS lane
- Stamp every document DRAFT, lawyer-review-required, not for execution; these accelerate counsel, they do not get signed.
- Cover the four cleanly: entity and IP checklist (formation plus clean IP assignment out of the personal estate), pilot term-sheet draft (pilot cost separated from any capital ask, plus a client-experience-protection clause), SAFE overview (with the honest note that rounds above roughly $5M flip toward priced equity, so instrument choice is counsel's call), and a contributor-agreement outline (milestone or deliverable-vested, founder control preserved, no fixed split).
- Flag the securities and sensitive-data pass and the dual-role conflict-of-interest disclosure as required before money moves.
- Separate what must be true THIS WEEK (entity plus IP assignment) from what can follow (SAFE, term sheet).
- **Most common failure mode:** a draft gets treated as executable and someone acts on it (assigns IP wrong, signs a term, promises a split) under deadline pressure without counsel. The stamp exists precisely to prevent this.

## 4. Naming and brand process for an enterprise AI, trust-positioned company in 2026

Prescriptive sequence:

1. **Territory before name.** Define 3 to 5 naming territories tied to the positioning (trusted companion, calm operating surface, attestation and origin, orchestration or cartography) and name the feeling each evokes. The founder should pick a direction before a word.

2. **Set 2026 trust-brand constraints up front.** Avoid names that overclaim autonomy or omniscience (registers like Omni, God-mode, Overlord, Autopilot spook enterprise buyers whose whole concern is control). Avoid "Agent" as the literal brand (category-generic, and a descriptor here). Avoid surveillance connotations. Favor calm, partnership, guidance, and human agency. Aim for 2 to 3 syllables, spellable and pronounceable on first hearing.

3. **Generate broad, then cut hard.** Produce 20 to 40 candidates across the territories using human plus model generation, then cut by the feeling and by the enterprise-buyer register, not by cleverness.

4. **Run a same-day knockout screen on the survivors (this is where most names die).** Trademark exact and phonetic search in classes 9 (software) and 42 (SaaS); live .com and .ai availability (a trust brand needs a real domain, not a workaround); GitHub, npm, and PyPI org names; social handles; a collision check against existing AI companies; and a "say it on a phone" test.

5. **Clear legally at trust-brand depth.** A knockout search now, a full attorney trademark-availability search before any spend, and an intent-to-use filing on the top one or two candidates before public launch. A trust-positioned company cannot afford to rename after launch.

6. **Run a linguistic and cultural check.** No unfortunate meanings in major languages, no negative connotations, and ideally it works as a verb.

7. **Present candidates with rationale and screen results to the founder, never a single ranked pick.** The founder decides. The moment he decides, lock the domain, handles, and trademark filing that same week.

8. **Reserve a descriptor line so the name need not explain itself** (for example "[Name], the trusted companion for the AI transition"). This frees the name to be evocative rather than literal, which is what a durable enterprise brand needs.

## 5. The 5 places polish tips into overclaim for AI startups, and the QA gate for each

1. **Roadmap rendered in the present tense.** Polish turns "will have multi-tenancy" into "has." Gate: a built-versus-designed tag on every capability claim; a reviewer may mark a claim "shipped" only if a repo, commit, or demo backs it. Externally, only Tier A and B capabilities speak in the present tense; the internal strength scorecard is the source of truth and never leaves the building.

2. **Multipliers as headlines.** Polish turns "4 matched the output of 18" into "16x." Gate: no raw multiplier as a headline; loop-deletion and honest-range framing instead (task-level 20 to 40 percent controlled, firm-level 5 to 15 percent). The 16x claim is barred from every external surface until the methodology memo lands, with the output-parity fallback prebuilt.

3. **Survey and vendor stats laundered into measured fact.** Polish drops the qualifier so "62 percent experimenting (survey)" becomes "62 percent of companies use agents." Gate: the MEASURED / SURVEY / VENDOR / [unverified] tag travels with every stat; a shared claims ledger sources each stat once; nothing external ever cites an [unverified] number.

4. **Demos implying a scale that does not exist.** Polish presents a fleet-of-one reference implementation as a multi-tenant product live at paying customers. Gate: every demo and proof is labeled reference implementation / fleet-of-one, and any fictional demo (for example a treasury desk) stays clearly fictional with no real client data. Never imply live multi-tenant paying usage that is not there.

5. **The ask inflating past the evidence.** Polish lets a scoped pilot plus a small check quietly read as a fundable large round. Gate: one canonical "The Ask" wording reused verbatim across the kit; a reviewer confirms no artifact implies a raise the evidence does not yet earn; and readiness scores never appear on a slide, since they are an internal prioritization tool only.

## 6. Single strongest conviction

**Engineer the entire kit around one act: show a known investor the thing already running, and ask for exactly what that demonstration earns.**

The unfair advantage is not the deck, the whitepaper, or the name. It is that Jarred already operates this product at fleet-of-one scale, every day, on a live streak. That is the one thing no competitor and no frontier lab can copy this quarter, and it is the only asset that turns "faith" into "achievement plus faith." So the highest-value hour is not spent polishing an artifact past honest and clear; it is spent making one real front door visibly close one real loop in the room, and sizing the ask to precisely that proof. If the kit had to be cut in half tomorrow, I would keep the live demonstration, the honest ICP and buyer-ROI, and the coherent scoped ask, and treat everything else as supporting cast. Conviction capital from people who know you is won by demonstrated achievement, not by a bigger number or a shinier deck. Build the frame around the demonstration, not the other way around.
