## Evidence

### A. Solo-operator / key-person risk (general startup evidence)

- Solo founders fail within the first two years at roughly 70%, versus about 40% for founding teams, and raise on average 60% less funding, with weaker terms and more difficulty at Series A and beyond, when investors expect proof of execution capability across multiple functions at once ([Hypertxt](https://www.hypertxt.ai/blog/marketing/why-solo-founders-fail), [Techstars](https://www.techstars.com/blog/pov/solo-founders-are-riskier-the-best-ones-prove-theyre-worth-the-bet)).
- A 2023 Mercer Marsh Benefits study found most respondent organizations expected to lose a key person within three years, and a majority predicted a "high" operational impact when that happened; this is the standard lens board members and investors now apply ([Beige Media](https://www.beigemedia.org/article/key-person-risk-single-point-of-failure)).
- In most startups in the $1-5M revenue band, the single point of failure is explicitly the founder: every deal, hire decision, and product call funnels through one person; illness, burnout, or a personal emergency can halt operations completely for a true solo operator ([Beige Media](https://www.beigemedia.org/article/key-person-risk-single-point-of-failure), [Bonny Morlak](https://www.bonnymorlak.com/post/single-points-of-failure-in-startups-the-hidden-risk-that-can-break-your-company)).
- "Bus factor" is now a standard, explicit investor due-diligence check in 2026: a company with bus factor 1 is described as harder to fund, harder to sell, and more likely to face painful pivots when the key person leaves; consequences of losing the sole technical person include an immediate stall (no one can continue meaningful work the next day), weeks-to-months of reverse-engineering undocumented decisions, and forced technical-debt rewrites ([FoundersBar](https://foundersbar.com/articles-and-research/bus-factor-explained-silent-startup-killer)).
- Countervailing 2026 narrative: solo-founder legitimacy is also rising, with AI-leveraged one-person companies cited approvingly by some investors, and Dario Amodei giving the first one-person billion-dollar company 70-80% odds within 2026 ([Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/05/18/solo-founders-ai-automation-entire-teams-entrepreneurs/)). This cuts against, not for, complacency here: the trend legitimizes lean solo *operators inside a company they fully own*, it does not resolve the bus-factor problem for a three-way, unincorporated partnership about to take outside capital, where "who actually holds this if Jarred is unavailable for six weeks" is precisely the question diligence will ask.
- This venture's specific exposure is compounded, not generic: the overview itself states the estate (claudecode-bar, the-sage, mini achievement engine, fieldy-clawdia, teamclaude-fleet) is "Jarred's own estate IS the product at fleet-of-one scale." That is the textbook bus-factor-1 case investors are now trained to flag.

### C. Three-way partnership, zero entity, zero IP agreement

- The core legal exposure: every individual owns their own work and its IP until formally assigned to a company, or until the company is incorporated and IP is assigned into it. Founder agreements should use present-tense assignment language ("hereby assigns"), not a future promise, and should be retroactive to pre-incorporation work ([HyperStart](https://www.hyperstart.com/blog/founders-agreement/), [Focus Law LA](https://focuslawla.com/ip-ownership-disputes-startup-partners-california/)).
- Courts have ruled in favor of founders who claimed personal ownership of work created when a governing agreement was silent on the question; under partnership law, jointly created IP can default to partnership property with equal rights for each partner absent a restricting agreement ([Focus Law LA](https://focuslawla.com/ip-ownership-disputes-startup-partners-california/)).
- Consequences when this surfaces late: unclear IP ownership is a major investor red flag that can delay or collapse funding rounds and acquisitions; VCs specifically want IP assignments from everyone who has ever worked on the company before committing capital ([Omni Legal Group](https://omnilegalgroup.com/blog/founder-agreements-ip-ownership-avoiding-disputes-before-they-start/), [Dhillon Law Group](https://www.dhillonlaw.com/behind-every-successful-startup-battle-ownership/)). One attorney's framing: "being a co-founder is much worse than getting married. There is no way to get divorced" without one ([Bloomberg Law](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/legal-exchange-insights-and-commentary/how-startup-founders-can-avoid-disputes-that-turn-into-lawsuits)).
- Standard prevention practice: form the entity early (cheap and fast), get signed IP assignment from all three founders, use standard vesting (one-year cliff, four-year vesting), and avoid a flat 50/50-style split without a documented rationale ([Promise Legal](https://promise.legal/startup-legal-guide/formation/equity-splits), [Dhillon Law Group](https://www.dhillonlaw.com/behind-every-successful-startup-battle-ownership/)).
- 2026 diligence environment specifically: most startup diligence runs 4-10 weeks, has gotten faster rather than lighter, and now expects a structured, README-led data room as a positive signal; compliance items once reserved for later rounds (SOC 2, AI bias audits) are increasingly expected even at seed ([Evalyze.ai](https://www.evalyze.ai/blog/startup-due-diligence)). A $10-50M ask and a separate equity-for-overhaul conversation with a federally chartered bank are exactly the deal sizes where this level of diligence should be assumed, not hoped against.
- Specific to this venture: the overview confirms the three founders currently hold clearly separable personal IP (Jarred's entire estate, Carter's Recurro framework and the 16x case study, Nick's game-design/tokenomics material) with no stated agreement covering how any of it converts into joint-venture IP, equity, or decision rights. This is the precise fact pattern (informal start, verbal-only understanding, no entity) that the legal research above identifies as the leading cause of founder lawsuits once money starts moving.

### D. Agentic-AI hiring market, July 2026

- Agentic-AI engineering hiring is described as a seller's market: demand has outrun a thin talent pool and pushed typical base pay to roughly $185K-320K; the average Agentic AI Engineer salary in the U.S. is about $190K, with top earners over $300K; AI Agent Development specifically pays $200-320K total comp and is growing 136% year over year ([KORE1](https://www.kore1.com/agentic-ai-hiring-2026/), [KORE1](https://www.kore1.com/ai-engineer-salary-guide/)).
- At frontier labs, including Anthropic (the model layer this estate runs on), senior agent-focused roles pay $300-550K total comp, and Anthropic itself is scaling its applied AI team 5x in 2026, competing directly for the same talent this venture would need ([KORE1](https://www.kore1.com/agentic-ai-hiring-2026/)).
- Demand signal: agentic-AI job postings grew 280% year over year to roughly 90,000 U.S. listings per Stanford's 2026 AI Index, and postings mentioning agentic-AI skills were up 986% between 2023 and 2024; 76% of employers still cannot fill open AI roles ([JobsByCulture](https://jobsbyculture.com/blog/agentic-ai-hiring-boom-2026), [Second Talent](https://www.secondtalent.com/resources/most-in-demand-ai-engineering-skills-and-salary-ranges/)).
- Firms offering below a $200K base salary floor for senior AI talent face a 114-day average time-to-fill, versus 52+ days for the broader tech market; AI-specific roles overall see 90-120 day time-to-fill and 40% fewer qualified applicants per posting than equivalent general software roles ([KORE1](https://www.kore1.com/agentic-ai-hiring-2026/), [Syndesus](https://syndesus.com/how-long-does-it-really-take-to-hire-an-ai-engineer-a-week-by-week-hiring-timeline-for-2026/)).
- Countervailing supply-side fact worth using as a mitigation lever: traditional software engineering hiring has contracted sharply in the same period, with programmer employment down 27.5% and entry-level tech hiring down 25% year over year ([JobsByCulture](https://jobsbyculture.com/blog/agentic-ai-hiring-boom-2026)). There is a large pool of strong, currently underemployed generalist engineers who are not competing in the narrow "agentic-native" bidding war and could be hired faster and cheaper, then ramped, rather than the venture only chasing the scarcest self-labeled specialist segment.
- Full-cycle hiring reality check for senior roles generally: 47 days average from requisition to offer acceptance is the optimistic case; senior/staff-level roles more typically run 60-90+ days because strong candidates are rarely actively looking, and full cycles including sourcing, 3-5 interview rounds, and notice periods can run 90-120 days ([Recruiting from Scratch](https://www.recruitingfromscratch.com/blog/how-long-does-it-take-to-hire-a-senior-software-engineer-in-2026), [Syndesus](https://syndesus.com/how-long-does-it-really-take-to-hire-an-ai-engineer-a-week-by-week-hiring-timeline-for-2026/)).

### E. Ramp cost beyond generic hiring time

- Beyond the hiring-market numbers above, this venture's specific ramp cost is higher than the generic figures suggest, because the reference implementation is not a conventionally documented codebase. Per the overview, the estate spans roughly 15 repos (claudecode-bar at 322 commits, the-sage at 58, skills-registry at 835, fieldy-clawdia at roughly 40 scripts, teamclaude-fleet, and others), with the primary internal documentation layer being doctrine-injection and memory-bank files explicitly designed to brief Claude agents and Jarred himself, not to onboard a new human engineer.
- Generic due-diligence and bus-factor literature converges on the same fix applicable here: a structured, README-led data room and explicit documentation of "the ugly version first" (recording how the task is actually done before handing it off) meaningfully de-risks this specific gap, and is cited as a positive signal to investors in its own right, not just an internal efficiency measure ([FoundersBar](https://foundersbar.com/articles-and-research/bus-factor-explained-silent-startup-killer), [Evalyze.ai](https://www.evalyze.ai/blog/startup-due-diligence)).

## Mitigations (consolidated)

1. **This week:** write down one explicit priority order for 7/9-7/18 in a single shared doc; protect specific Anchorage-delivery hours pitch prep cannot touch, given the client-experience law cannot be traded away.
2. **This week:** start the entity formation and founders' agreement process (roles, vesting, IP assignment in present-tense "hereby assigns" language, equity split) in parallel with pitch prep. This is a 1-2 week legal exercise with a startup attorney and does not block the 7/17 date if started now; starting it after a term sheet or signed pilot is the failure mode the legal evidence above warns against.
3. **Before 7/15:** move Carter's case-study deadline earlier (e.g., 7/13) to create real review buffer before the deck locks and before the pitch; treat 7/15 as rehearsal, not first draft.
4. **Before the pitch:** be explicit and unembarrassed in the room that gamification mechanics are "in design, spec due 7/31," rather than letting the gap surface as a surprise gap under questioning.
5. **If the raise or equity-pilot closes:** budget 4-6 months of hire-ahead runway explicitly, and broaden the hiring funnel to strong generalist engineers (currently in oversupply) with a deliberate onboarding path, rather than competing purely for scarce self-identified "agentic-native" specialists; use fractional/contract engineers as an immediate bridge while a full-cycle search runs.
6. **Independent of any raise:** commission a human-facing architecture/onboarding document for the estate now, separate from the agent-facing memory-bank and doctrine files, and get a key-person insurance quote and a written emergency-continuity plan on file before the pitch. Both are cheap, fast, and directly defuse the two sharpest diligence questions ("what if Jarred is unavailable" and "who owns what") a serious investor or partner will ask.

## What would change the assessment

- **Lower the risk:** an entity forms and a founders' agreement with IP assignment is executed before 7/17; Carter or Nick take on a real, verifiable share of Anchorage delivery labor (not just parallel doc-writing) during Sprint 1/2; a second engineer, even part-time or contract, is confirmed working inside the actual estate (not just briefed on it) before the pitch; the Anchorage relationship's actual current status gets documented and confirmed rather than remaining an open thread.
- **Raise the risk:** any sign of slippage on the 7/15 concept doc or case study this close to the pitch; any visible degradation flagged by Anchorage during Sprint 1 (which would trip the one explicit inviolable constraint); the pitch proceeds without any entity or written founder terms and a term sheet or signed pilot arrives before those are in place; Carter's or Nick's deliverables continue to be the only evidence of their contribution with no shared repo/doc access or joint authorship trail that would support an IP claim later.
