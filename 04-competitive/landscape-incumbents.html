Landscape — incumbents

Category: incumbents

Compiled: 2026-07-08

Method: live web research (WebSearch), 2025-2026 sources prioritized. Time-boxed pass, not exhaustive.

Read against: research/synthesis/product-concept-overview-2026-07-08.md (7-pillar Agent OS concept: calm conversational front door, OKR-to-agent-subtask alignment scoring, gamified adoption, meeting-capture intelligence, consultative delivery as moat, skill marketplace, proof-of-origination).

Headline

Every incumbent in this set is racing to own agent orchestration, and several (Salesforce, ServiceNow, Microsoft) are already selling agent seats as "digital labor" at real revenue scale. But none of the nine players combines all three of our differentiating bets: native OKR-to-agent-subtask alignment scoring, a productized gamified-adoption layer sold as a feature, and a direct (not-through-a-reseller) consulting+equity delivery motion. Gamification shows up everywhere as an internal change-management trick vendors use on themselves or pitch as a services idea (Microsoft's own Copilot Expo, a Salesforce blog post, a ServiceNow community app, PwC's internal program) but nobody ships it as a governed, anti-abuse, points/leagues product surface. OKR software specifically is a receding category at the platform layer: Microsoft killed Viva Goals outright (retired December 31, 2025, no replacement, customers told to go find a third-party tool) at the exact moment agentic AI took off. The nearest thing to our skill-marketplace pillar already exists and is live money: Salesforce's AgentExchange charges $0.10 per agent action via Flex Credits and just took a $50M operator fund. The single biggest real threat is not any one feature match, it's speed and distribution: Microsoft, Salesforce, ServiceNow, and Atlassian each have the org-wide surface area, the enterprise sales motion, and (in three of four cases) an internal case study proving gamified/agentic adoption works, that they could bolt an OKR+gamification layer onto their existing agent orchestration in a single roadmap cycle if a customer conversation forced the question.

Per-player analysis

Microsoft (Copilot / Copilot Studio / Agent 365)

Orchestration is real and getting more capable fast: Work IQ API (public preview, April 2026) adds built-in orchestration, and agent-to-agent (A2A) communication now lets agents delegate as peers with shared org context. Agent 365 (GA May 1, 2026) is a dedicated governance/observability control plane for IT and security leaders, bundled into the new Microsoft 365 E7 tier ($99/user/month) alongside E5, Copilot, and Entra. Pricing is credit-metered ($200 per 25,000 Copilot Credits/month) on top of per-user licensing ($18-30/user/month), and a 5-agent HR/IT/sales/service/ops deployment realistically runs $3,000-6,000/month in credits alone before per-user seats.

The sharpest finding: Microsoft retired its own OKR product. Viva Goals (the Ally.io acquisition) was shut down entirely on December 31, 2025, with Microsoft stating outright it will not replace it or fold the functionality into another product, and directing customers to third-party OKR tools. That is a live, unforced admission that platform-native OKR software didn't reach scale for them, at the exact moment they were building the agent orchestration that our concept wants to wire to OKRs.

On gamification, Microsoft has the single best proof point of anyone in this set that it works: an internal "Copilot Expo" used points, badges, avatars, and a leaderboard to drive Copilot adoption, and Microsoft's own research claims fun/gamification lifts engagement 24%, productivity 50%, and cuts habit-formation time 40%. But this is an internal HR event, not a shipped product feature customers can buy or configure. Consulting-hybrid: Microsoft doesn't do this itself, it sells through partners (Avanade, Accenture, Wipfli, Centric) under a partner-capability-score program; Accenture's own 743,000-seat internal Copilot rollout is cited as proof of scale, but that's Accenture buying and reselling, not Microsoft doing direct consultative delivery for equity.

Salesforce (Agentforce / AgentExchange)

Agentforce is explicitly framed as "digital labor," now at $125-550/user/month on top of three overlapping pricing models shipped in 18 months ($2/conversation, then Flex Credits at $0.10/action, then per-user). Traction is real but rockier than the marketing: 5,000 deals but only 3,000 paid, under 10% of customers scaled past pilot, and production takes 5-11 months with $50k-150k upfront plus $10-25k/month ongoing consulting. A credible competitor, Sierra AI, is taking share with pure outcome-based pricing ($100M to $150M+ ARR by early 2026, $10B valuation), which is a signal the market is unhappy with Salesforce's consumption-pricing opacity.

AgentExchange is the most direct existing analog to our skill-marketplace pillar: in 2026 Salesforce merged AppExchange, Slack Marketplace, and Agentforce into one storefront (10,000+ apps, 1,000+ pre-built agents, 2,600 Slack apps), backed by a $50M operator fund, with per-action Flex Credit pricing that is functionally pay-per-skill-run already live today. Consulting-hybrid is Salesforce's most mature dimension of any player here: a formal Forward Deployed Engineering Partner Network launched with Accenture and Deloitte plus 30+ firms (PwC, Slalom, IBM, Capgemini, KPMG, TCS), and that network already drives one-third of all successful Agentforce implementations. The difference from our model: Salesforce captures platform economics and lets SI partners capture the relationship and consulting margin; nobody at Salesforce is doing the operator's direct high-trust cash+equity delivery motion.

Google (Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform, formerly Agentspace/Vertex AI)

The most technically sophisticated orchestration story: Google rebranded and unified Vertex AI + Agentspace into "Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform" (GA April 22, 2026), with a code-first Agent Development Kit (Python/Go/Java/TypeScript) supporting graph-based multi-agent supervision and a now-production Agent2Agent (A2A) protocol for cross-platform agent communication. Pricing is pure usage-metering with no flat subscription, spread across 15+ separately billed services (Agent Engine runtime, session/memory storage, Vertex AI Search, per-model tokens, and soon Agent Gateway billing from July 13, 2026, and Memory Bank billing from September 1, 2026), which one analysis flags as a real cost-opacity risk since a single request can silently trigger charges across three or four services.

Nothing found on OKR features, gamification, or a consulting-hybrid identity: Google Cloud's enterprise motion runs through Google-certified system integrator partners, arms-length from the platform team. Weakest enterprise-traction signal of the big three cloud/SaaS players in this search pass, more infrastructure-analyst coverage than case-study coverage.

ServiceNow (AI Agents, AI Agent Orchestrator, AI Control Tower)

ServiceNow's own product is literally named "AI Agent Orchestrator," which coordinates agent teams, reroutes failed/low-confidence work to another agent or a human queue, and was ranked #1 by Gartner's 2025 Critical Capabilities report for building/managing AI agents. The February 2026 "Autonomous Workforce" push, built on the $2.85B Moveworks acquisition, claims 90%+ IT ticket auto-resolution and is the closest positioning of anyone here to "we run your organization's work for you." A sweeping April 9, 2026 pricing overhaul replaced 5 legacy tiers with 3 AI-native ones (Foundation/Advanced/Prime), bundling Now Assist, Moveworks, and AI Control Tower into every tier as a competitive move against Salesforce's still-separate AI add-on pricing; only the Prime tier builds custom agents.

Gamification exists only at the community/store level (a third-party "GAMIFICATION" app on the ServiceNow Store; gamified ServiceNow University training) not as part of the AI-agent adoption story itself. No OKR-native tie found. The consulting-hybrid overlap is real but structural rather than intentional: ServiceNow's own cost data shows license spend is only ~25% of total cost of ownership, with $3-5 of implementation/tuning spend for every $1 of license, meaning the business is already effectively consulting-hybrid economics, just captured by SIs, not ServiceNow directly, and definitely not for equity.

Atlassian Rovo

The strongest enterprise-adoption numbers found in this entire pass: 5 million+ monthly active users, used by 75% of Fortune 500 and 90%+ of Atlassian's enterprise customers, agentic automation up 7x in six months, 14 million assisted actions in a single month, and 2.4 million workflow automations across the customer base in the last six months. Team '26 (May 5-7, 2026) shipped Agents in Jira to general availability: Rovo Agents can now be assigned issues, mentioned in comments, and embedded directly into workflows with full audit logging, plus a no-code Rovo Studio (GA) for building custom agents and a "Max mode" that breaks complex requests into multi-step plans executing across tools with human-in-the-loop only at decision points.

This is the sleeper threat to pillar 2. Jira already is where a huge share of the market decomposes objectives into epics/stories/tasks, and Confluence/Jira Goals already track OKR-like structures organically; now that Rovo agents can be assigned and can execute those tickets directly, Atlassian is one product decision away from formal OKR-to-agent-subtask scoring, riding an install base most competitors would need years to build. No gamification signal found. No consulting-hybrid identity: Atlassian's model stays PLG plus admin-driven rollout, with a much thinner solution-partner services layer than Salesforce or ServiceNow.

ClickUp (Brain / Brain² / Super Agents)

Directly relevant because ClickUp is already the ops backbone inside the operator's own estate. ClickUp already ships native Goals (Objectives -> Targets, with 4 KR types and auto-updating progress percentages) plus Super Agents that can autonomously triage, reassign, and report against those goals in natural language, and role-based agents (Product, Finance, Design, Ops, Research, Project, People) marketed as a "company AI" that self-improves and routes to the best model, an almost one-to-one echo of our own skill-registry self-improver framing. Reviewers are clear the OKR side is still shallow versus a dedicated OKR tool (no check-ins, no goal-level comments, no health dashboard), which is exactly the gap our alignment-scoring pillar targets.

Pricing has generated real backlash: Brain is billed per paid workspace seat regardless of who actually uses AI (a 30-person team paying for 5 users of Brain still pays for all 30), which is a trust/perception problem an alternative could exploit. No gamification signal found, no consulting-hybrid arm; ClickUp is pure self-serve SaaS.

Linear

Positioned furthest from whole-org OS ambition of anyone in this set (engineering-only, smallest company), but two details are worth tracking. First, the CEO has publicly declared "issue tracking is dead" and is repositioning around Linear Agent (public beta, March 2026), which is now embedded in Slack/Teams/Zendesk, understands roadmap/issues/code, and can be asked to save any successful workflow as a reusable "Skill" for future reuse, an internal, non-monetized preview of our skill-marketplace idea without the marketplace or payment layer. Second, coding agents (including third-party ones like Claude Code, Devin, Cursor, Copilot) already sit inside 75% of Linear's enterprise workspaces as first-class assignable teammates, and agent-driven work volume is up 5x in three months, real evidence that "assign work to an agent like a teammate" is already normal in at least one vertical. No OKR (Linear's Projects/Initiatives are roadmap containers, not KR-scored), no gamification, no consulting motion.

Notion AI

Notion Agent (personal, works autonomously up to 20 minutes) and Custom Agents (scheduled/triggered, credit-metered at $10/1,000 credits since May 2026) give Notion a credible generalist agent layer with wide MCP-based integrations (Linear, Figma, HubSpot, Stripe, GitHub, and more), and users can already pick between GPT-5.2, Claude Opus 4.5, and Gemini 3 per task. This is the closest thing among the smaller players to our calm-front-door idea ("meet your AI team"/"24/7 AI team" messaging), but it is workspace-general rather than org-hierarchy-aware: no OKR product, no gamification, no consulting arm, and Enterprise pricing is custom/compliance-gated (SCIM, audit logs, zero data retention) rather than a distinct delivery motion.

Slack (Agentforce-in-Slack / Slackbot-as-orchestrator)

The most direct existing analog to pillar 1 (the calm conversational front door): Slackbot has been repositioned as a universal MCP client that can route any request to Agentforce or any other agent/app in the enterprise, so "employees never need to know which system handles which task." The Agentforce Hub inside Slack is effectively an agent directory/marketplace UX (browse agents, start chats, view history) sitting inside the one surface employees already use daily, and AI-enabled Slack apps have grown 690% year over year. But this is a Salesforce-owned extension, not an independent play: reviewers note Agentforce agents "remain tied to a single system of record," so Slack's front-door ambition currently still resolves back into Salesforce's data model rather than acting as a neutral, org-wide brain. No OKR, no gamification, no independent consulting motion beyond Salesforce's own.

What nobody does / whitespace

  1. OKR-to-agent-subtask alignment scoring as a native, live product feature. The category is actually shrinking at the platform layer, not growing: Microsoft killed its own OKR product (Viva Goals, retired 12/31/2025) with an explicit no-replacement statement. Jira/Rovo and ClickUp Goals are the closest partial fits (both can now route agent-executed work against goal-like structures) but neither scores per-subtask weighted alignment against a Key Result the way our pillar 2 describes; both are described by reviewers/practitioners as shallow relative to dedicated OKR tooling.
  2. Gamified AI adoption sold as a governed product, not a one-off event. Every strong data point (Microsoft's Copilot Expo, Salesforce's Simantel case study post, ServiceNow's community gamification app, PwC's 300,000-person internal program) is either an internal culture exercise, a blog post, or a third-party marketplace add-on. Nobody in this set ships points/tiers/streaks/leagues/anti-abuse as a first-party, admin-configurable layer bolted to their agent product. Given how strong the proof points already are (24%/50%/40% Microsoft numbers; 30-40% adoption lift and 25% data-quality lift cited for gamified Salesforce rollouts), this is a real, provable, currently-unclaimed feature gap.
  3. Direct consulting+equity delivery as the vendor's own moat. Salesforce, ServiceNow, and Microsoft all run services-heavy economics (Salesforce implementations run $50-150k plus $10-25k/month; ServiceNow's TCO is 75% services; Microsoft's biggest proof point is Accenture's own deployment) but in every case a third-party SI captures the relationship, the trust, and the margin. None of the nine takes equity, none does hands-on operator-level delivery, none treats the consulting relationship itself as the primary moat the way our concept does.
  4. Anti-abuse/anti-gaming mechanics for agent-usage scoring. PwC's "governance guardrails to prevent low-value AI consumption" is the only gesture found toward this; no platform publishes caps, outlier detection, or role-adjusted scoring for agent/token usage.
  5. Proof-of-origination / attribution for agent-originated work. Zero evidence found anywhere in this pass. This mirrors our own scorecard (this is our own weakest, least-built pillar too), so it remains wide open, but also unproven as a market need by any incumbent's roadmap signal.
  6. "Reward, not replace" as explicit branded philosophy. Every vendor here frames agents as headcount-saving "digital labor" (Salesforce's own word) or ticket-deflection (ServiceNow's 90% auto-resolution claim). Nobody is messaging gamified adoption as an explicit anti-fear, retention-linked employee pact the way our concept states it ("reward employees... versus people being worried about losing their job").
