## The framing said out loud

Three ways this meeting can end. All three are fine with us, and we're saying so up front. A decision this size deserves room, not a corner.

## Branch A: the pilot

You take the operator seat. Ninety days, two phases, instrumented before day 1: a signed baseline, a named owner, a written kill criterion. Capture plus one gated workflow in the first 30 days; game mechanics for an opt-in cohort only after the day-30 evidence clears.

The strategic check isn't pressed on this branch. One sentence covers it: the pilot generates the evidence, and the ownership conversation follows the evidence.

## Branch B: the pilot plus a smaller strategic check

Everything in Branch A, plus the owner seat, taken from strength. This is the earliest entry this will ever offer and the lowest base it will ever have: an accretive position priced by proof, not a favor to anyone. Early, low base, evidence-gated. That's the whole case.

Two disciplines, stated in the room.

1. Two papers, never blended. The check buys no pilot concessions, no exceptions to the gates, no special data access. The pilot fee buys no investment terms.
2. Instrument and size aren't negotiated in the meeting. Both are counsel and founder territory, settled off the table after the securities pass.

## Branch C: a pass, and why that's respected

Staying in your lane is a discipline we'd rather see honored than argued with. Only accretive moves, no scope creep: if this doesn't clear your bar today, passing on it IS the discipline this whole document praises, and we'll say that to your face.

What happens next on this branch is specific, not polite. You keep the document and the standing pilot structure; it doesn't expire. We go sign the first design partner elsewhere, and when the first day-30 gate clears, you see the evidence package, not a new pitch. Your next look is at measured results.

A pass costs the relationship nothing. The door stays open at the price of proof, and proof is what we'll bring.

## The hurdle, worked

One calculation, on the page, because adjectives aren't arithmetic. Every input is a candidate until the baseline is signed.

- Recovered executive hours: the reference implementation band is 3-6 hours per week (MEASURED, n=1; generalization to any buyer [unverified] until the two-week time audit runs). Over the 13-week pilot: 39 to 78 hours.
- Those hours get dollarized ONLY at the buyer's own stated rate, agreed at kickoff, or reported raw. Illustration at $1,000/hr [unverified illustration; the buyer's number governs]: $39K to $78K of recovered time against a pilot fee in a working band of $45-60K [unverified; founder sets it; not quotable until counsel and founder clear the terms]. The hours alone roughly return the fee at mid-band.
- The hurdle is a double, matching the discipline of doubling a baseline yield. The second half doesn't come from hours. It comes from what the buyer keeps: a production workflow behind gates, follow-up debt driven toward zero from its day-0 count (reference end state MEASURED, n=1), and the head start itself.

Same-budget-line check: a full-time chief of staff runs $240-265K+ per year true cost, about $60-66K for the same 90 days, and a fractional one $7-14K per month (both VENDOR, secondhand). The pilot's working band sits at or under the budget line the role already occupies, with the software seat and the gates included.

If the audit says the hours aren't there, the calculation says don't do it. That's the point of writing it down.
