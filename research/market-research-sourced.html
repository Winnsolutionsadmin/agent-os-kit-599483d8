1. Market Size — Enterprise AI / Agentic AI 2025–2027

Gartner

  • Agentic AI spending in software markets projected to reach $201.9B in 2026, overtaking chatbot spending by 2027. Long-term: agentic AI software spend projected to hit $985B by 2030 (62.7% CAGR 2025–2030). — Gartner: Forecast Analysis, Agentic AI Spending in Software Markets
  • Broader AI spend context: total AI spending reaches $2.53T in 2026, $4.71T by 2029 (Gartner). ⚠ WEAK sourcing on this specific figure — it's aggregated via secondary roundup, not a primary Gartner press release; treat as directional. — Roundup of agentic AI forecasts, 2026
  • 40% of enterprise apps will feature task-specific AI agents by 2026, up from <5% in 2025. — Gartner press release, 2025-08-26
  • Adoption is still early: only 17% of organizations have deployed AI agents to date (2026 Gartner CIO Survey), though >60% expect to within two years. — Gartner 2026 Hype Cycle for Agentic AI
  • Failure risk: Gartner predicts >40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end of 2027 (cost, unclear value, weak risk controls) — this is directly relevant as a "why our governed control loop matters" data point. — same Hype Cycle source above.
  • ⚠ CONTESTED / scope-dependent: standalone "agentic AI market" sizing varies 25x depending on definition — Fortune Business Insights ($7.29B in 2025 → $139.2B by 2034), Precedence Research ($7.55B → $199.05B by 2034), MarketsandMarkets ($7.06B → $93.2B by 2032). These are much smaller than Gartner's figures because Gartner counts agentic capability embedded across *all* enterprise software spend, not a standalone category. Do not average these with Gartner's numbers — they are not measuring the same thing.

IDC

  • Global enterprises will invest $307B on AI solutions in 2025, rising to $632B by 2028. — IDC FutureScape / AI & GenAI Predictions
  • Agentic AI-enabled spend drives 31.9% YoY growth 2025–2029, reaching $1.3T in 2029; agentic AI will exceed 26% of worldwide IT spending by then. — IDC press release
  • AI infrastructure spend (a different, narrower bucket — compute/hardware, not software/services): $318B full-year 2025 (2x 2024's $153B), projected $487B in 2026, >$1T by 2029. — IDC blog, Q4 2025 infra spend
  • Note for the deck: IDC's $307B (2025) figure is *all enterprise AI spend* (infra + software + services), not agentic-specific — use it as top-of-funnel TAM context, not a direct agentic-AI number.

McKinsey — "The State of AI in 2025: Agents, Innovation, and Transformation" (Nov 2025; survey fielded Jun–Jul 2025, n=1,993, 105 countries, 38% from $1B+ revenue orgs)

  • 88% of companies use AI regularly in ≥1 function (up from 78% in 2024); 72% report gen-AI use (up from 33% in 2024).
  • 23% of orgs report scaling an agentic AI system somewhere in the enterprise; another 39% are experimenting. In any *given* business function, no more than 10% are scaling agents — i.e., "scaling" claims are mostly narrow/departmental, not org-wide. This is a load-bearing stat for the "no one has cracked whole-org agent orchestration yet" wedge claim.
  • 39% say AI has moved company EBIT, but in most cases it's <5% of total EBIT. Only ~6% of respondents qualify as "AI high performers" (>5% of EBIT attributable to AI, high value capture).
  • High performers are 3x more likely to report strong leadership commitment from the top, and ~33% of them allocate >20% of digital budget to AI vs. 7% of everyone else.

— McKinsey, State of AI 2025 / full PDF

Takeaway for the deck: Use Gartner's $201.9B (2026) agentic-software figure and IDC's $307B (2025) all-AI-enterprise-spend figure as the two headline TAM anchors — they're primary-sourced and dated. Do NOT quote the sub-$10B "standalone agentic AI market" figures (Fortune BI, Precedence, MarketsandMarkets) alongside Gartner's without flagging the scope mismatch — an investor who knows the space will catch it.

2. Forward-Deployed-Engineer Model Economics (Palantir) — Precedent for the "Chief of Staff Seat" Lane

  • Palantir's FDE model is explicitly not consulting in its own framing — FDEs sit upstream of the product roadmap doing product discovery/formation embedded at the client, vs. traditional consultants who operate downstream of a signed SOW. This is the closest real-world precedent to a "chief-of-staff seat that also shapes the product."
  • Margins: 88% adjusted gross margin, 60% adjusted operating margin; GAAP operating margin roughly doubled Q1'25→Q1'26 (~20%→~46%). Q1 2026 revenue growth 85% YoY — fastest since IPO.
  • Bootcamp scaling mechanism: Palantir compresses a 12–18 month sales cycle into a 5-day AIP bootcamp with >75% conversion and >$1M initial deal size — this is the direct comparable for "chief-of-staff seat → land fast, expand." — Forbes, Palantir and FDE, 2026-07-10
  • FDE unit economics (industry-wide, not Palantir-specific): fully-loaded cost per FDE at a frontier lab ≈ $845K/year midpoint (comp $350–756K + ~30% benefits/tooling/travel/overhead). Anthropic's equivalent "Applied AI Engineer" median total comp: $582,500. Palantir's own baseline FDE comp is lower (~$238K, senior staff >$630K) — Palantir is comparatively capital-efficient here. FDEs reportedly generate $3–15M in annual revenue contribution at frontier labs (3–15x fully-loaded cost) on large accounts, but the same model loses money against smaller clients — directly relevant to minimum-deal-size discipline in Proj-X's chief-of-staff lane. — MindStudio, Digitech Bytes FDE Economics 2.0
  • ⚠ CONTESTED accounting critique: some FDE cost sits in R&D/S&M rather than COGS — reclassified, gross margin would be materially lower, i.e. the market may be pricing Palantir as software when the underlying unit economics are services-like. Professional-services peers run ~18-20% of revenue as a cost ratio; if commercial-scale growth requires proportional FDE headcount, margin expansion claims don't hold. — Digitech Bytes
  • Frontier labs copying the model: Anthropic and OpenAI both launched FDE-style enterprise embedding efforts (~May 2026). ⚠ WEAK signal on OpenAI specifically: reported that OpenAI guaranteed investors in its deployment vehicle a 17.5% annual return, a structure typical of capital-intensive/lower-margin businesses — some read this as smart money pricing "AI deployment" as services, not recurring SaaS. Treat as a single reported data point, not confirmed financials.

3. Pricing Comparables By Revenue Lane

(a) Retained AI chief-of-staff / operator seat — testing the $20–40k/mo anchor

Published fractional-executive benchmarks are lower than the $20–40k/mo anchor at the median, but the top of the range supports it for near-full-time, senior, AI-specialized engagements:

Verdict: $20–40k/mo is defensible but top-quartile, not median — it requires framing as "senior, AI-specialized, near-full-time, embedded" (which matches Proj-X's chief-of-staff-seat positioning) rather than a generic fractional-exec comparison. Cite the $15K–$40K AI-specialist band, not the $6K–$14K generalist median, and say so explicitly to a sophisticated investor.

(b) Engineering-org overhaul engagements

  • MBB (McKinsey/BCG/Bain) blended rates: $500–$1,000+/hr; senior partners $1,100–$1,200/hr for strategy. Big 4: $300–$600/hr. Mid-tier (Accenture, IBM, Capgemini): $250–$500/hr. — rate guide aggregation
  • Full transformation engagements: $500K entry point up to $5M+ for multi-year, multi-BU programs at Big4/MBB tier. Illustrative team math: 6-person team, blended $300/hr, 40hr/wk, 20 weeks = $1.44M. — [same source]
  • Enterprise retainers with Big 4 for dedicated teams: $50,000–$150,000/month.
  • AI-specific consulting (non-MBB) 2026 benchmarks: mid-market program $35K–$150K; enterprise AI transformation typically starts at $500K, frequently exceeds $1M; ongoing retainers $15K–$100K/month for dedicated team access. — Bosio Digital
  • ⚠ WEAK/single-outlet claim: "$900/hr for AI engineers deployed as consultants" (Fortune, Sept 2025) — treat as a scarcity-premium anecdote, not a market rate.
  • No published "agentic engineering transformation" specific rate card exists yet — this is a genuine white space (2026 market data confirms the term is emerging but unpriced), which is itself a point Proj-X can make: it's early enough to help set the market rate rather than compete against an established one.

(c) Per-org / per-seat software (agent platform ACVs)

Seat-based comparables:

  • Microsoft 365 Copilot: $30/seat/mo add-on (annual commit); real all-in cost $66–$90/seat/mo once required E3/E5 base license is included. 1,000-seat org = $360K/yr for the add-on alone. New E7 "Frontier Suite" bundle (M365 E5 + Copilot + Agent 365 control plane) launches $99/seat/mo May 2026. — Microsoft pricing, Velosio calculator
  • GitHub Copilot Enterprise: $39/seat/mo (+ required Enterprise Cloud $21/seat/mo → real cost ~$60/mo). — GitHub
  • Glean: no public pricing; base license ≈$50/user/mo median reported (range $10-50 depending on volume); 100-seat minimum (~$50K-$60K minimum ACV); full TCO for mid/large deployments $350K-$480K/yr once infra, support fee (~10% of ACV), and paid POC (up to $70K) are included. — GoSearch, Vendr
  • Devin (Cognition): Pro $20/seat/mo, Max $200/seat/mo, Teams $80/mo base + $40/mo/seat, Enterprise custom (ACU-based). Large orgs with custom deployment reportedly pay $100K+/yr. Cognition's ARR grew $1M → $73M in 9 months after the 2025 price restructure (illustrates how fast agent-platform ARR can move once pricing/product-market-fit lands). — Devin pricing, VentureBeat
  • Sierra: no public pricing; third-party estimates: contracts start ~$150K/yr, Year-1 (with onboarding/rollout) often $200K-$350K+; setup fees $50K-$200K. Outcome-based (per resolved conversation) — company crossed $150M+ ARR by Feb 2026. — Sierra blog, Fin.ai analysis
  • Salesforce Agentforce: $2/conversation list price (customer-facing agents only), or Flex Credits ($500/100K credits, ~$0.80-$1.50/effective conversation), or per-user licensing from $125/user/mo. 50,000 conversations/yr ≈ $100K. — Salesforce pricing
  • General enterprise AI agent deployment cost (proxy for whole-system ACV, not just seats): average cost to deploy an enterprise AI agent system fell to $276,000 in 2026 (down from $358K in 2025, $512K in 2024) as tooling matured. — aggregated 2026 market report
  • Market-level: global spend on enterprise AI agent platforms projected $47.8B in 2026, up from $28.1B in 2025 (+70% YoY). ⚠ WEAK — this figure comes from a secondary aggregator, not a named analyst firm; use directionally only.

Verdict on per-org software ACV: A $200K–$400K/year whole-org software ACV (post chief-of-staff/overhaul phases) sits squarely inside the observed range of comparable platforms (Glean's $350-480K TCO, Sierra's $200-350K Year 1, average $276K deployment cost) — this is a well-supported anchor, better supported than either the $20-40K/mo seat price or the multi-million-dollar overhaul engagement.

4. The Adoption Gap As the Monetizable Wedge

This is the strongest, most heavily corroborated section — multiple independent firms converge on the same story: capability is outpacing adoption, and the gap is organizational, not technical.

  • MIT NANDA, "The GenAI Divide: State of AI in Business 2025" (July 2025; 52 exec interviews, 153-leader survey, 300 public deployments analyzed): 95% of enterprise GenAI pilots show no measurable P&L impact despite $30-40B invested. Only ~5% achieve rapid revenue acceleration. Root cause named explicitly as "the learning gap" — tools that can't retain feedback/adapt to context — an organizational/process failure, not a capability failure. Buying from vendors + partnering succeeds ~67% of the time vs. internal builds succeeding at ~1/3 that rate. 90% of workers use personal/shadow AI tools daily even though only 40% of companies have official LLM subscriptions — evidence people want the capability but official rollout is bottlenecked. This single stat move markets: it reportedly contributed to a pullback in AI-exposed public equities (Palantir, Nvidia, Oracle, CoreWeave) when published. — Fortune coverage, full PDF
  • BCG, "The Widening AI Value Gap" (Sept 2025) (1,250 senior execs surveyed): 60% of companies report "hardly any material value" from AI investment; 74% show no tangible value overall; only 5% are "future-built" (extracting value at scale) and they get 5x the revenue lift / 3x the cost reduction of everyone else. Maturity breakdown: stagnating 14%, emerging 46%, scaling 35%, future-built 5%. Diagnosis: BCG's "10-20-70 rule" — AI success is 10% algorithms, 20% data/tech, 70% people/process/culture. This is close to a direct restatement of Proj-X's thesis (governed control loop + adoption mechanics as the actual bottleneck) from a top-3 strategy consultancy. — BCG report, full PDF
  • RAND Corporation (2025): 80.3% of enterprise AI projects fail to deliver promised business value (33.8% abandoned pre-production, 28.4% reach production but miss expectations, 18.1% never recover the investment) — only 19.7% deliver on the business case. ⚠ Cited via secondary aggregation in the BCG search result — verify against RAND's own publication before quoting in the deck; treat as directionally consistent with MIT/BCG but not yet primary-sourced here.
  • Gartner: 30% of GenAI projects abandoned after POC by end of 2025; >40% of agentic AI projects specifically will be canceled by end of 2027 (escalating cost, unclear value, weak risk controls) — same citation as Section 1.
  • Deloitte: only 21% of companies have mature governance for agentic AI in 2026 — cited as a hidden-cost/risk factor layering on top of headline consulting bill rates. ⚠ WEAK — picked up via secondary aggregator, not Deloitte's own report; re-verify before using as a headline stat.

Cross-check consistency: MIT (95% no ROI), BCG (60-74% no material value), Gartner (30-40% project cancellation) are three independent methodologies converging on the same conclusion — enterprise AI has a large, well-documented adoption/scaling gap, not a capability gap. This triangulation is the strongest evidentiary claim in this entire brief and should headline the "why now" section of the deck.

5. Bottom-Up Path: 1 Org → 10 → 100

Using the pricing comparables above, a defensible (not fantasy) per-org revenue ladder:

1 → 10 → 100 math (illustrative, not a commitment):

  • 1 org, blended Lane A+B+C in year 1 of relationship: plausible $800K-$2M total contract value across the three lanes (seat retainer + overhaul engagement + emerging software subscription), consistent with the comparables above.
  • 10 orgs (proof-of-repeatability stage): if Lane C software ACV is the sustaining recurring layer at $200K-$300K/yr blended average (conservative, mid-point of Glean/Sierra/average-deployment-cost comparables), 10 orgs ≈ $2-3M ARR from software alone, plus lumpy Lane A/B services revenue on top (services should be modeled as front-loaded/decaying per org as software takes over — this mirrors Palantir's own stated bootcamp strategy of compressing services time to accelerate toward software economics).
  • 100 orgs: $20-30M ARR from Lane C at the same $200-300K blended ACV — this is the tier where the model needs to show services-to-software mix shifting hard toward software (Palantir-style), because 100 orgs' worth of chief-of-staff-seat services revenue at $20-40K/mo does not scale operationally without an FDE-style compute/leverage story (this is the single biggest execution risk in the whole model, and it's the same risk profile analysts flag for Palantir's FDE economics in Section 2).
  • What's defensible: ACV assumptions in the $150K-$400K/yr recurring-software range per org, because multiple independent comparables (Glean, Sierra, general agent-deployment-cost data) converge there.
  • What's fantasy without more evidence: (1) assuming Lane A/B services margins scale linearly with org count — the FDE literature explicitly says they don't without heavy productization; (2) assuming enterprise agentic-AI market growth rates (Gartner's 62.7% CAGR) translate 1:1 into Proj-X's own bookings growth — market tailwind ≠ company-specific capture rate, and Gartner's own data shows 40%+ of agentic AI projects get canceled, which will also hit Proj-X's logo base.

Weakest Links In This Brief (flagged explicitly for the investor conversation)

1. Standalone "agentic AI market size" figures vary by 25x across analyst firms depending on scope definition — pick one framing (Gartner's embedded-in-enterprise-software view) and stick to it; do not blend.

2. The RAND 80.3%-failure and Deloitte 21%-governance-maturity figures were surfaced via secondary aggregators in this research pass and have not been verified against the primary RAND/Deloitte publications — re-verify before they appear in an investor deck.

3. "$900/hr AI consultant" and "$1.06B Caixa gamification profit" figures are single-case anecdotes, not market benchmarks — fine as color, not as a pricing justification.

4. No market data exists yet for "agentic engineering transformation" as a named, priced consulting category — this is either a genuine white-space opportunity (bull case) or evidence the category doesn't have enough proof points to price confidently yet (bear case). The deck should pick a framing and defend it rather than let it sit ambiguous.

5. Gamification/adoption-mechanics ROI stats (20-60% productivity gains, 700% profit upside) are heavily vendor-blog-sourced; treat as supporting color for "adoption mechanics work when well-designed," not as a quantitative input to revenue modeling.
