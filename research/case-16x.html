The 16× case, shown.

The throughput claim gets repeated across this room, so this page holds it to diligence standards: the definition, the math, the controls, the caveats, and what remains available under NDA. Client and team identities stay withheld; the evidence does not.

The claim, precisely

  • One adopting team (4 engineers) reached approximately 16× its own pre-adoption throughput baseline at peak (June, actual measurement, not a projection).
  • Cycle time fell from roughly 35 days to a ~5-day median (~7× faster).
  • A same-company, same-period control team (18 engineers) on the same market and tools moved between 1–4× over the same window.

Trajectory, as measured

Methodology, all of it

  • Throughput definition: issues closed + pull requests + commits, measured against each team’s own pre-adoption baseline over the same repositories. No industry strawman, no cross-team comparison of absolutes.
  • The honest normalization: the adopting team is small (4 engineers), so its baseline is small. Normalized per engineer against the 18-engineer control, the gain is approximately 2× per engineer. Both numbers are shown everywhere the claim is made; the 16× is a team-vs-its-own-baseline multiple, not a per-person miracle.
  • The control: same company, same period, same market pressure, same underlying tools. The variable was the operating layer, not the toolchain.
  • Quality guard: output counted only when merged and released through the same review gates that existed pre-adoption; the review gate was not loosened during the window.
  • Reproduction: the control team subsequently adopted the same playbook and is climbing a comparable ramp. The pattern has now run twice, across two organizations and two toolchains.

What this does not claim

  • It does not claim 16× for every team, or for large teams: small baselines amplify multiples, which is exactly why the per-engineer figure travels with it.
  • It does not claim the result is fully attributable to software alone; the operating layer arrived with process change, and the two are sold together on purpose.
  • It does not name the organizations or their commercial terms.
